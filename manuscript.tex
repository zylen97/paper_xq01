\begin{abstract}
Improvisation capability enables project teams to respond effectively to unexpected contingencies, yet the cognitive mechanisms through which digital communication tool use shapes this capability, particularly the role of meta-knowledge, remain underexplored. Drawing on distributed cognition theory, this study develops and tests a model linking work-oriented and social-oriented digital communication tool use to improvisation capability through three dimensions of meta-knowledge: structural, relational, and procedural. Three-wave time-lagged survey data from 255 large-scale construction project teams in China reveal that the two usage orientations cultivate distinct yet complementary forms of meta-knowledge, which selectively transmit effects to the two dimensions of improvisation capability. Structural meta-knowledge consistently mediates both usage orientations and both capability dimensions; relational and procedural meta-knowledge exhibit selective effects. The findings advance understanding of how digital communication tool use shapes team cognition in project settings and offer practical guidance for designing meta-knowledge-oriented digital workspaces.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Large-scale construction projects, characterized by long cycles, complex technical interdependencies, and multiple-stakeholder involvement \citep{yang2025How}, often encounter contingencies \citep{shen2022Largescale}. Traditional plan-driven project management approaches are challenged by the need for quick responses to contingencies \citep{malucelli2021Facing,coulon2026Wing}. Project teams' improvisation has emerged as a critical ability for addressing these challenges \citep{sumbal2025Improvisation}. \textbf{\textit{Improvisation capability}} refers to the ability to spontaneously create and implement solutions to unexpected project contingencies by rapidly mobilizing and recombining existing knowledge and resources \citep{magni2009Role,veranghg2005Improvisation}. For instance, the final joint of the Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macao Bridge immersed tunnel exceeded its acceptance tolerance. In response, the project team improvised an unprecedented reverse-operation to re-dock the structure, reducing the deviation from 17 centimeters to 2.5 millimeters within 38 hours \citep{21cbh2018Redocking}. The convergence of planning and execution in time and the generation of novel responses distinguish improvisation capability from routine problem-solving \citep{moorman1998Organizational, miner2001Organizational}. Enhancing project teams' improvisation capability is of practical importance, especially given the difficulty of comprehensive planning in a temporary organization setting \citep{wang2025Formalization}.

Existing project management research on antecedents of improvisation capability in project teams has concentrated on three areas, as shown in Table 1. First, cognitive and psychological states, such as collective mindfulness \citep{jiang2025Confronting} and psychological safety \citep{huang2026Thriving}, are shown to enable improvisation capability. Second, the knowledge and experience base, including tacit knowledge and sensemaking \citep{sumbal2025Improvisation} and ambidextrous search \citep{rui2022Contingent, klein2015Improvisation}, contributes to improvisation capability development. Third, contextual and environmental antecedents, such as project complexity \citep{mamedio2020Managing}, plan deviations \citep{vuorinen2019Lifecycle}, and uncertainty \citep{hacker2019Legitimizing}, prompt improvisational responses. \textbf{\textit{Yet, limited study has examined the role of communication technology through which existing knowledge is shared and mobilized}}, which is crucial for improvisation formation \citep{veranghg2005Improvisation}.

Effective improvisation depends on the real-time mobilization of distributed knowledge \citep{magni2009Role, moorman1998Organizational}. Compared with general projects, large-scale construcion projects involve a greater number of participating organizations \citep{hu2024Performance}, deeper cross-disciplinary knowledge heterogeneity \citep{yang2025How}, and wider geographical dispersion of work sites \citep{shen2022Largescale}. These characteristics amplify the challenge of real-time knowledge mobilization. When unexpected events arise, conventional communication mechanisms, such as scheduled meetings and formal documentation, cannot operate at the speed that improvisation demands. \textbf{\textit{Digital communication tools (DCTs)}} are well positioned to address this problem. As the primary medium to coordinate and exchange expertise in real time, DCTs enable team members to instantly reach across organizational and geographical boundaries to locate relevant knowledge and mobilize it for immediate action \citep{zhang2018Mobile}. In practice, mobile social media platforms such as WhatsApp, Microsoft Teams, WeChat, and Slack have become common for cross-organizational coordination, mediating task assignment, technical problem-solving, and informal exchange \citep{ma2022Investigating, jia2025Knowledge}. However, the project management literature has not examined whether and how DCT use contributes to the project team's improvisation capability. 

Moreover, DCT use in project teams is not a homogeneous behavior. Team members use DCTs both for work-focused coordination, such as assigning work, solving technical problems, and tracking progress, and for social-oriented exchange, such as sharing experiences, seeking informal advice, and maintaining interpersonal connections. Prior research in the construction project context has confirmed this distinction, showing that work-oriented and social-oriented use patterns produce different effects on project outcomes \citep{ma2022Understanding, jiang2026Bolstering}. Yet how these distinct usage patterns contribute to improvisation capability remains unexplored. \textbf{\textit{Therefore, the first aim of this study is to examine how work-oriented and social-oriented DCT use influence improvisation capability in large-scale construction projects.}}

A critical problem facing project managers, then, is through what cognitive mechanism these DCT use patterns translate into their teams' improvisation capability. In large-scale construction projects, DCTs generate a substantial volume of information flows across organizational boundaries \citep{treem2013Social}. These flows increase the knowledge density accessible to team members \citep{leonardi2015Ambient}. Improvisation demands that relevant expertise be located and mobilized within extremely compressed time frames. Without team-level awareness of who holds what knowledge and how it can be accessed, project teams risk being overwhelmed by abundant but undirected information \citep{sun2021Dark}. They cannot convert the knowledge available through DCTs into timely improvised action. This cognitive resource---the project team's collective awareness of how knowledge is distributed and accessible within the team---is what the literature terms \textbf{\textit{meta-knowledge}} \citep{wegner1987Transactive, leonardi2014Social}. Project management literature has predominantly examined knowledge itself, focusing on the sharing, transfer, and integration of knowledge as enablers of project performance \citep{jia2025Knowledge, hu2024Performance}, while paying limited attention to meta-knowledge. Without understanding how project teams develop their members' awareness of knowledge distribution and access, the literature cannot explain the cognitive foundation of improvisation capability. Research in information systems has demonstrated that enterprise social media can enhance meta-knowledge through communication visibility \citep{leonardi2014Social, engelbrecht2019How}. Yet the project management field has not examined how DCT use cultivates this cognitive resource, nor how different usage patterns may do so through distinct pathways. \textbf{\textit{Therefore, the second aim of this study is to examine how meta-knowledge serves as the cognitive mechanism through which work-oriented and social-oriented DCT use influence improvisation capability.}}

This study investigates how work-oriented and social-oriented DCT use influence improvisation capability through the development of meta-knowledge in large-scale construction projects. We integrate the characteristics of large-scale construction project teams (highly dispersed expertise, limited shared work history, and time-compressed coordination) with the meta-knowledge literature \citep{wegner1987Transactive, leonardi2014Social}. On this basis, we examine three dimensions of team meta-knowledge: structural meta-knowledge (knowing who knows what), relational meta-knowledge (knowing who knows whom), and procedural meta-knowledge (knowing how to access and mobilize knowledge). We test the proposed model using three-wave survey data from 255 large-scale construction project teams in China. This study draws on distributed cognition perspective \citep{hutchins2020Distributed}, which conceptualizes cognition as emerging from interactions among individuals, technological artifacts, and the social environment. This perspective is suited to our context because it treats DCTs not as passive channels but as active constituents of the cognitive system through which meta-knowledge is developed and improvisation capability is enacted \citep{leonardi2015Ambient}. Thus, this study answers the following two research questions (RQs):

\textbf{RQ1:} \textit{How do work-oriented and social-oriented digital communication tool use influence improvisation capability in large-scale construction project teams?}

\textbf{RQ2:} \textit{Does project teams' meta-knowledge mediate the relationship between digital communication tool use and improvisation capability?}

The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. Section 2 presents the theoretical foundations and develops the research hypotheses. Section 3 describes the methodology. Section 4 presents the results. Section 5 discusses the findings, answers to RQs, and research implications. Section 6 concludes with limitations and directions for future research.

\section{Theoretical foundations and hypotheses development}
\subsection{Improvisation capability in large-scale construction projects}
The concept of improvisation originated from analogies between jazz performance and organizational action, with early work defining improvisation primarily as the convergence of planning and execution \citep{moorman1998Organizational}. Subsequent studies further reconceptualized improvisation as a process in which existing resources and knowledge are creatively recombined to produce novel responses under constraints \citep{miner2001Organizational, crossan2003Making}. Integrating both the temporal and knowledge-recombination perspectives, \citet{veranghg2005Improvisation} and \citet{magni2009Role} operationalized improvisation capability as comprising two dimensions: \textbf{\textit{immediacy and creativity}}. \textbf{\textit{Immediacy}} captures the capacity to formulate and implement solutions with minimal delay between conception and action \citep{veranghg2005Improvisation}. \textbf{\textit{Creativity}} reflects the ability to generate novel solutions through non-routine recombination of available knowledge and resources, producing outcomes that go beyond established procedures \citep{magni2009Role}. This suggests that effective improvisation requires not merely speed of response, but also the quality and originality of the solution produced under temporal constraints. 

In the project management domain, improvisation capability has received growing scholarly attention, particularly in large-scale construction project contexts. Table~\ref{tab:antecedents} synthesizes the existing research on antecedents of improvisation capability in the project management literature, including three categories. First, cognitive and psychological states, demonstrate that conditions such as collective mindfulness \citep{jiang2025Confronting, wang2025Formalization}, psychological safety \citep{huang2026Thriving}, and facilitating conditions \citep{coulon2026Wing} create a state of readiness that enables spontaneous action. Second, knowledge and experience base establishes that improvisation draws on existing knowledge, including tacit knowledge \citep{sumbal2025Improvisation}, bricolage \citep{xie2025How}, ambidextrous search \citep{rui2022Contingent}, and theoretical knowledge foundations \citep{klein2015Improvisation, nisula2016Role}. Third, contextual and environmental triggers, identify conditions such as project complexity \citep{mamedio2020Managing, jiang2023Toward}, plan deviations \citep{hamzeh2018Investigating, vuorinen2019Lifecycle}, and extreme uncertainty \citep{hacker2019Legitimizing, malucelli2021Facing} that necessitate improvisation. 

All three streams do not examine the infrastructure through which knowledge is located and mobilized during improvised action. Psychological readiness does not automatically translate into coordinated action across organizational boundaries. Possessing knowledge is not equivalent to mobilizing it under temporal constraints. The absence of communication technology from existing antecedent research is notable given the centrality of DCTs in contemporary large-scale construction project practice. DCTs have become the primary medium through which team members coordinate tasks, share technical knowledge, and reach across organizational boundaries in real time \citep{zhang2018Mobile, jia2025Knowledge}, constituting the primary infrastructure for cross-boundary knowledge mobilization \citep{ma2022Investigating}. An examination of how DCT use shapes improvisation capability is therefore warranted.

\subsection{Digital communication tool use in project teams}
DCT research in organizational settings has established that visibility, persistence, editability, and association of DCTs can transform communication from information exchanges into persistent and collectively visible traces \citep{treem2013Social}. These characteristics create ambient awareness of distributed activities and expertise \citep{leonardi2014Social}. Empirical project management work has demonstrated the centrality of DCTs. \citet{zhang2018Mobile} showed that mobile social media platforms such as WhatsApp serve as primary tools for cross-organizational virtual collaboration in inter-organizational projects. \citet{jia2025Knowledge} found that mobile social media groups have become the dominant channel for knowledge sharing within project teams. \citet{ma2022Investigating} demonstrated that DCT-mediated communication facilitates cross-boundary coordination and collaborative problem-solving in rojects. These studies establish that DCTs have been embedded in the daily work of construction project teams.

Research on technology use has increasingly distinguished between work-oriented and social-oriented patterns \citep{ali-hassan2015Linking, cao2019Exploring}. Work-oriented use refers to purposeful, task-driven interactions such as searching for technical information, assigning tasks, discussing project problems, and coordinating schedules \citep{ma2022Investigating}. Social-oriented use refers to relationship-driven interactions such as sharing personal experiences, seeking informal advice, exchanging emotional support, and maintaining interpersonal connections \citep{ali-hassan2015Linking}. In construction project settings, these two patterns produce distinct effects. Work-oriented use primarily enhances task performance, while social-oriented use strengthens relational coordination \citep{ma2022Understanding, jiang2026Bolstering}. These differential effects are likely amplified in large-scale construction projects, where more pronounced cross-organizational boundaries and wider geographical dispersion heighten the need for both task coordination and relationship maintenance. How work-oriented and social-oriented use patterns influence the cognitive resources that underpin improvisation capability in project teams remains unexplored.

\subsection{Meta-knowledge in project teams}
Meta-knowledge, rooted in transactive memory systems, refers to an organization's collective awareness of how knowledge and expertise are distributed, connected, and mobilized within the organization \citep{wegner1987Transactive}. In the transactive memory systems literature, meta-knowledge originated as the cognitive component that enables team members to locate relevant expertise without possessing it themselves \citep{lewis2003Measuring, kush2019Conceptual}. 

Three research streams contribute to the understanding of meta-knowledge. First, transactive memory systems research established that effective team coordination depends on members' awareness of how expertise is distributed across the team \citep{wegner1987Transactive, lewis2003Measuring}. This dimension refers to ``knowing which team members hold which domain-specific knowledge'' \citep{kush2019Conceptual, ellwart2014Team}. We term this content-based awareness \textbf{\textit{structural meta-knowledge (SM)}}: the project team's collective awareness of how expertise and knowledge domains are distributed across team members, enabling efficient identification of appropriate knowledge sources. 

Second, social network and communication visibility research has demonstrated that organizations also develop awareness of the relational pathways through which knowledge can be accessed \citep{borgatti2003Relational, leonardi2014Social}. Studies of social media have shown that communication visibility enables individuals to perceive not only who knows what but also who knows whom, identifying the interpersonal ties and intermediaries through which expertise can be reached \citep{fu2025Enterprise, engelbrecht2019How}. We term this awareness \textbf{\textit{relational meta-knowledge (RM)}}: the project team's collective awareness of social ties and interaction pathways through which distributed knowledge can be accessed. 

Third, team coordination research has highlighted that team members also develop awareness of how distributed expertise is typically mobilized and combined in practice \citep{faraj2000Coordinating}. The coordination dimension of transactive memory systems \citep{lewis2003Measuring, kush2019Conceptual}, interaction molding routines in complex project work \citep{korotkova2024Do}, and meta-knowledge of organizational processes \citep{barmeyer2019Informal} all point to a process-oriented facet of meta-knowledge. We term this action-oriented awareness \textbf{\textit{procedural meta-knowledge (PM)}}: the project team's collective awareness of how distributed expertise is typically mobilized, combined, and enacted, including cross-disciplinary problem-solving routines and established approaches for responding to contingencies.

Synthesizing these three streams, this study adopts a three-dimensional conceptualization of meta-knowledge encompassing SM, RM, and PM. Empirical meta-knowledge work has been conducted almost exclusively within stable organizational teams, such as R\&D groups, management teams, and entrepreneurial ventures \citep{mohammadparsttabas2024Igniting, ren2011Transactive, kush2019Conceptual}, where membership continuity allows knowledge structures to gradually stabilize. Research situated in project management contexts, particularly large-scale construction projects, remains scarce. Large-scale construction project teams face conditions that make meta-knowledge particularly important yet difficult to develop \citep{korotkova2024Do}. This study thus proposes that meta-knowledge serves as the mediating mechanism through which DCT use influences improvisation capability.

\subsection{Distributed cognition perspective}
The distributed cognition perspective provides the theoretical foundation for this study. Originating from Hutchins' studies of navigation teams, this perspective advances three core propositions \citep{hutchins2020Distributed, hollan2000Distributed}. First, cognitive processes are distributed across organizations, artifacts, and the environment. Second, technological artifacts are active participants in cognitive processes, shaping how information is represented, stored, and retrieved. Third, the appropriate unit of analysis for understanding cognition is the functional system (the ensemble of people, tools, and representations engaged in a task) rather than the isolated individual.  

Recent distributed cognition studies have examined how digital artifacts structure and redistribute cognitive processes in design teams \citep{mangalaraj2014Distributed, nemeth2004Using} and how team cognition co-evolves with cognitive artifact use in project management \citep{drury-grogan2021Changes}. These propositions are relevant to large-scale construction project teams, where knowledge is dispersed across organizational boundaries and DCTs serve as the primary artifacts through which coordination and knowledge mobilization occur \citep{leonardi2015Ambient, safadi2024Balancing}. By treating DCTs as elements of the cognitive system rather than mere communication channels, the distributed cognition perspective offers a theoretical basis for this study.

Within this framework, meta-knowledge functions as a form of cognitive indexing within the distributed system, enabling team members to navigate the knowledge landscape without possessing all relevant expertise themselves \citep{engelbrecht2019How, ye2021Leveraging}. As DCTs become embedded in project work, interaction processes that were previously transient or implicit are externalized through persistent communication traces, visible interaction histories, and routinized collaboration structures \citep{leonardi2014Social, treem2020ComputerMediated}. These externalized traces constitute the informational foundation from which meta-knowledge can develop and be maintained \citep{palazzolo2006Coevolution}. Research suggests that the development of meta-knowledge is closely linked to improvisational action: when team members possess well-developed meta-knowledge, they can rapidly identify, access, and recombine distributed expertise to formulate improvised responses \citep{huang2017Ties, oostervink2016Knowledge}. \subsection{Hypotheses development}
\subsubsection{Work-oriented digital communication tool use and meta-knowledge}
Work-oriented use externalizes task-related contributions into accessible cognitive traces, including technical discussions, problem-solving records, and project coordination logs \citep{hadida2015Use, jiang2013Should}. These traces encode content-based information about contributors' expertise domains \citep{lewis2003Measuring}. In large-scale construction projects, members distributed across multiple organizations and work sites cannot directly observe each other's competence through co-located interaction. The cognitive traces externalized through work-oriented use become the primary window into cross-boundary expertise distribution. Through both task-focused search and ambient exposure to accumulated work records \citep{leonardi2015Ambient}, team members can observe what types of expertise different contributors demonstrate and map substantive knowledge domains to specific actors. This content-based expertise mapping supports the development of SM. Accordingly, we propose:

\textbf{H1a}: Work-oriented use is positively associated with SM.

Work-oriented use also makes task interaction patterns visible through traceable digital records: who responds to whom, how requests are routed, and which actors coordinate across tasks \citep{borgatti2003Relational, leonardi2017Social}. These externalized interaction patterns reveal the actual coordination relationships that emerge from task execution, enabling team members to infer interpersonal pathways for accessing knowledge beyond what formal organizational charts prescribe \citep{ding2019Enterprise, leonardi2015Ambient}. In large-scale construction projects where formal authority across organizational boundaries provides limited guidance for knowledge access \citep{shen2022Largescale}, the coordination relationships made apparent through work-oriented use render the relational landscape of the project team legible. By observing recurring patterns, project members can develop awareness of which interpersonal ties serve as effective channels for reaching expertise. Thus, we propose:

\textbf{H1b}: Work-oriented use is positively associated with RM.

Work-oriented use externalizes work processes into cognitive traces that constitute what the distributed cognition perspective terms externalized cognitive representations \citep{faraj2000Coordinating}. Task sequence records, coordination histories, and problem-resolution trajectories accumulate as reviewable artifacts within the distributed cognitive system. In large-scale construction projects, formal process documentation is often incomplete or fragmented across organizational boundaries \citep{yang2025How}, as participating entities maintain different procedural standards and documentation practices. The task execution traces externalized through work-oriented use serve as a procedural cognitive foundation. This allows team members to observe how similar tasks were previously processed, how coordination sequences unfolded, and how problems were resolved. Team members accordingly develop awareness of procedural patterns and action sequences without relying on prolonged shared experience. Therefore, we propose:

\textbf{H1c}: Work-oriented use is positively associated with PM.

\subsubsection{Social-oriented digital communication tool use and meta-knowledge}
Complementary to the task-cognition externalization of work-oriented use, social-oriented use externalizes social interactions into perceivable relational traces via DCTs \citep{ali-hassan2015Linking, cao2019Exploring}. Through social interactions, team members gain exposure to colleagues' personal backgrounds, informal expertise developed outside current project tasks, and tacit knowledge that remains invisible in formal work exchanges \citep{ma2022Investigating}. Unlike the task-performance signals externalized by work-oriented use, social exchanges reveal experiential and contextual information about competence domains that extend beyond formally assigned roles. In large-scale construction projects, cross-organizational boundaries create information silos about expertise distribution \citep{shen2022Largescale}; social-oriented use provides a channel through which members can discover expertise that would otherwise remain hidden within organizational boundaries. As these social cues accumulate, they enrich and refine members' awareness of how expertise is distributed across the team. Accordingly, we propose:

\textbf{H2a}: Social-oriented use is positively associated with SM.

Social-oriented use externalizes relational structures into perceivable interaction records through informal exchanges \citep{huang2017Ties}. Co-engagement patterns, trust-based ties, and intermediary connections become discernible as social interactions accumulate within DCTs. The distributed cognition perspective holds that the cumulative and traceable nature of DCT-mediated interaction converts transient social exchanges into traceable relational information \citep{leonardi2014Social}. Unlike the formal coordination patterns revealed by work-oriented use, these traces reflect trust-based relationships and personal connections that operate outside established reporting structures. In large-scale construction projects, formal authority structures spanning multiple participating organizations provide limited guidance for cross-boundary knowledge access \citep{shen2022Largescale}. Social-oriented use enables project team members to discover and map informal relational pathways, identifying which interpersonal ties serve as effective channels for reaching distributed expertise. Therefore, we propose:

\textbf{H2b}: Social-oriented use is positively associated with RM.

Social-oriented use also externalizes procedural cognition through experience sharing, practical advice, and situational discussion \citep{ali-hassan2015Linking}. These social exchanges provide narrative and judgment-based insights into how work is actually carried out in practice, including tacit norms, preferred approaches to handling recurring issues, and situational judgments about when procedural deviations are acceptable \citep{barmeyer2019Informal}. From a distributed cognition perspective, these procedural cues complement the explicit process traces generated by work-oriented use. Whereas work-oriented use captures sequential task execution patterns, social-oriented use reveals the informal rules of thumb and contextual adaptations that team members apply when formal procedures encounter practical constraints. In large-scale construction projects, formal procedural documentation is often fragmented across participating organizations. Standardized protocols may not fully capture the cross-organizational coordination practices that emerge in actual project delivery. The procedural insights externalized through social-oriented use fill these gaps, helping team members develop awareness of how distributed work processes operate beyond what formal documentation prescribes. Accordingly, we propose:

\textbf{H2c}: Social-oriented use is positively associated with PM.

\subsubsection{Meta-knowledge and improvisation capability}
SM, RM, and PM function as different cognitive indexing that enable the project team to navigate the distributed knowledge landscape without possessing all relevant expertise themselves \citep{engelbrecht2019How, ye2021Leveraging}. Improvisation demands that distributed knowledge be mobilized within compressed timeframes to produce effective responses to unexpected situations \citep{magni2009Role}. The cognitive indexing provided by these three dimensions of meta-knowledge determine both the speed with which relevant knowledge can be located and mobilized, and the breadth of knowledge resources available for recombination. These two mobilization characteristics correspond to the two dimensions of improvisation capability: immediacy and creativity \citep{veranghg2005Improvisation}.

SM enables the project team to identify who possesses relevant domain-specific expertise. In large-scale construction projects, expertise distribution spans multiple organizations and work sites, and the team’s own knowledge is often insufficient to address emergent cross-disciplinary issues. By clarifying the content-based distribution of expertise across team members, SM reduces the cognitive search costs required to locate appropriate knowledge holders \citep{lewis2003Measuring}, enabling rapid identification and mobilization of relevant expertise under time pressure.

SM can also facilitate creative responses by allowing the project team to mobilize diverse knowledge domains beyond its immediate task scope \citep{ye2021Leveraging}. In large-scale construction projects, the heterogeneous expertise distributed across disciplinary and organizational boundaries constitutes a pool for knowledge recombination. When team members possess awareness of this expertise distribution, they can identify and integrate knowledge elements from different domains, generating solutions that transcend the boundaries of any single discipline. Thus, we propose:

\textbf{H3a}: SM is positively associated with immediacy.

\textbf{H3b}: SM is positively associated with creativity.

RM provides awareness of who is connected to whom, which individuals serve as intermediaries, and how indirect ties can be activated in practice \citep{huang2017Ties}. This pathway-based awareness shortens the gap between problem recognition and coordinated action \citep{faraj2000Coordinating}. In large-scale construction projects where formal authority across organizational boundaries is limited, RM enables the project team to bypass formal channels and activate interpersonal pathways to reach relevant expertise rapidly.

RM also supports creative responses by facilitating targeted access to knowledge holders across professional boundaries \citep{faraj2000Coordinating}. From a distributed cognition perspective, awareness of interpersonal pathways allows team members to selectively activate specific relational ties to reach expertise holders in different disciplinary domains \citep{perry-smith2017Creativity, soda2019Network}. This targeted cross-boundary interaction exposes team members to different professional logics and knowledge elements outside their own disciplinary repertoire. In large-scale construction projects, RM provides the pathway awareness necessary for team members to reach across organizational and disciplinary boundaries, supporting the recombination of heterogeneous inputs into novel action plans. Accordingly, we propose:

\textbf{H4a}: RM is positively associated with immediacy.

\textbf{H4b}: RM is positively associated with creativity.

PM provides familiar action templates that allow the project team to translate situational cues into action without deliberation \citep{turner2022Improving}. From a distributed cognition perspective, PM reduces uncertainty about ``how to act'' by making established action patterns cognitively available as ready-made frameworks for rapid deployment \citep{faraj2000Coordinating}. In emergent situations, delays often stem from uncertainty about how to proceed rather than from a lack of expertise \citep{crossan2003Making}. PM addresses this uncertainty by providing procedural frameworks, bypassing the need to construct action plans from scratch. In large-scale construction projects, coordination complexity amplifies procedural uncertainty. Team members with developed procedural awareness can draw on established coordination sequences to initiate rapid responses, even in unfamiliar situations.

PM supports creative responses by making familiar action patterns available as building blocks for recombination \citep{ciborra1996Improvisation}. When existing procedures prove insufficient for addressing unprecedented contingencies, project teams with well-developed procedural awareness can adapt, hybridize, and recombine known routines to construct novel approaches. Cross-disciplinary procedural awareness in large-scale construction projects expands the templates available for creative recombination, enabling members to draw on a wider range of procedural elements when composing improvised responses. Therefore, we propose:

\textbf{H5a}: PM is positively associated with immediacy.

\textbf{H5b}: PM is positively associated with creativity.

\subsubsection{The mediating role of meta-knowledge}
DCTs provide the informational foundation for meta-knowledge development. Meta-knowledge, functioning as cognitive indexing, in turn enables rapid mobilization of distributed knowledge to support improvisation capability. Temporary team composition and limited shared work history suggest that team members cannot rely on gradually accumulated familiarity to locate and mobilize distributed expertise \citep{jiang2025Confronting}. Meta-knowledge therefore works as the cognitive bridge between the information generated through DCT use and the capacity for improvised action. SM is expected to function as the most consistent mediator, because the visible and retrievable cognitive traces generated by both work-oriented and social-oriented use most directly support content-based expertise mapping, which in turn facilitates both rapid knowledge mobilization (immediacy) and cross-boundary knowledge recombination (creativity).

Based on the direct-effect arguments developed above, SM could mediate the work-oriented use--improvisation capability relationship because the task-content traces generated by work-oriented use provide the informational foundation for content-based expertise mapping, which in turn supports both rapid knowledge mobilization and cross-boundary knowledge recombination. RM mediation operates because work-oriented use externalizes task interaction patterns that reveal coordination relationships, enabling pathway-based knowledge access. PM mediation operates because work-oriented use externalizes sequential process traces that make procedural patterns cognitively available as action templates \citep{zhang2018Mobile}. Accordingly, we propose:

\textbf{H6a}: SM mediates the relationship between work-oriented use and improvisation capability.

\textbf{H6b}: RM mediates the relationship between work-oriented use and improvisation capability.

\textbf{H6c}: PM mediates the relationship between work-oriented use and improvisation capability.

For social-oriented use, SM mediation operates because social-oriented use externalizes informal cognitive cues that complement content-based expertise mapping from a different angle than work-oriented use. RM mediation operates because social-oriented use directly externalizes relational cognition, making trust-based pathways and interpersonal connections observable as cognitive artifacts \citep{nisula2016Role}. PM mediation operates because social-oriented use externalizes informal procedural insights from social exchanges, providing tacit norms and practical adaptations that complement formal process awareness. Therefore, we propose:

\textbf{H6d}: SM mediates the relationship between social-oriented use and improvisation capability.

\textbf{H6e}: RM mediates the relationship between social-oriented use and improvisation capability.

\textbf{H6f}: PM mediates the relationship between social-oriented use and improvisation capability.

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Data collection}
The unit of analysis in this study is the large-scale construction project team. Each respondent served as the informant reporting on their project team's practices and capabilities. Data were collected from participants involved in large-scale construction projects in China, spanning infrastructure projects, public buildings, residential buildings, and industrial buildings. The formal survey was distributed through a Chinese construction training organization that provides government-recognized certification programs for project professionals. The organization's trainee and alumni network includes practitioners from diverse organizational roles and project types, providing access to a broad and heterogeneous sample. Fig.~2 illustrates the overall data collection process. 

\begin{center}[Insert Fig. 2 here]\end{center}

Respondents were required to meet three eligibility criteria: (1) involvement in a project with a total investment of at least US\$100 million, consistent with the definition of large-scale construction projects proposed by \citet{harrison2024Scale}; (2) at least three months of involvement in their current project; and (3) regular use of digital communication tools for project collaboration. In the Chinese construction context, teams predominantly rely on three digital collaboration platforms, Feishu, WeChat, and DingTalk, for daily coordination and communication \citep{zhu2023Dynamics, jia2025Knowledge}. The survey therefore assessed respondents' tool use specifically within these three digital environments.

The formal data collection was conducted between March and August 2025 using a three-wave time-lagged survey design to reduce common method bias \citep{podsakoff2003Common}. At Time 1, respondents reported demographic information and DCT use patterns (work-oriented use and social-oriented use). A total of 467 questionnaires were distributed, and 369 valid responses were returned, yielding an effective response rate of 79.0\%. One month later, the Time 2 survey was administered to respondents who had provided valid responses at Time 1. This wave measured meta-knowledge variables, including structural, relational, and procedural meta-knowledge. A total of 284 valid responses were collected at Time 2, representing a retention rate of 77.0\% relative to the Time 1 sample. After another month, the Time 3 survey measured improvisation capability, including immediacy and creativity. To match responses across the three waves, respondents provided the last four digits of their mobile phone numbers as a unique identifier. After matching all three survey waves and excluding cases with missing or inconsistent data, the final sample comprised 255 valid responses, representing an overall retention rate of 54.6\% from Time 1. Respondents who completed all three waves received a gift as compensation for their continued participation. Table 2 presents the demographic information of the respondents. The sample is characterized by substantial project experience, with 69.4\% of respondents having more than ten years of work experience, and diverse professional roles spanning project managers, department managers, and professional engineers. 

\begin{center}[Insert Table 2 here]\end{center}

To ensure data quality, several measures were implemented. First, the survey company conducted IP address checks to prevent multiple submissions and removed responses with straight-lining patterns or unusually short completion times. Second, an attention check question was embedded in the survey to verify that respondents were reading the questions carefully. 

\subsection{Bias mitigation and evaluation}
Three procedural remedies were adopted to mitigate common method bias \citep{podsakoff2003Common}. First, all respondents were informed about the purpose of the study and assured that their responses were anonymous and would be used solely for academic research; informed consent was obtained prior to data collection. Second, the three-wave design ensured that independent variables, mediators, and dependent variables were measured at separate time points, preventing respondents from inferring the expected relationships among constructs. Following prior research on project-based organizations, a one-month interval was adopted between consecutive survey waves. Research suggests that a time lag of one to six months is appropriate for capturing behavioral and cognitive changes in organizational settings \citep{carmeli2021Resilience, zaman2024Stress}. In this context, one month was deemed sufficient for DCT use patterns to shape meta-knowledge through repeated daily interaction and coordination cycles. Third, survey instructions explicitly stated that there were no right or wrong answers and encouraged respondents to answer based on their actual experiences.

Two statistical tests were also conducted to evaluate the severity of common method bias. First, Harman's single-factor test was performed by subjecting all 24 measurement items to an unrotated principal component analysis. The results yielded seven factors with eigenvalues greater than 1.0, with the first factor accounting for 32.38\% of the total variance, well below the 40\% threshold \citep{podsakoff2003Common}. Second, a confirmatory factor analysis compared the hypothesized seven-factor measurement model (see Section 4.1) with a single-factor model in which all items loaded onto one latent factor. The single-factor model exhibited significantly poorer fit ($\chi^{2}$(252) = 1802.07, CFI = 0.496, TLI = 0.448, RMSEA = 0.155, SRMR = 0.124), and the chi-square difference test confirmed the superiority of the seven-factor model ($\Delta\chi^{2}$(21) = 1418.28, p < 0.001). Together, these results suggest that common method bias is unlikely to pose a serious threat to the validity of the findings.

To examine potential non-response bias, the final sample was divided into early and late respondent groups based on the order of Time 1 questionnaire receipt \citep{armstrong1977Estimating}. Independent-samples t-tests and chi-square tests were conducted to compare the two groups on key demographic variables. The results indicated no significant differences between early and late respondents in gender ($\chi^{2}$(1) = 0.476, p = 0.490), age (t = $-$0.502, p = 0.616), educational level ($\chi^{2}$(3) = 1.662, p = 0.645), or work experience (t = 0.055, p = 0.957). These results suggest that non-response bias is not a significant concern in this study.

\subsection{Questionnaire development}
Questionnaire development followed a three-step procedure based on \citet{anderson1988Structural}. First, building on a systematic review of peer-reviewed literature, established measurement scales were identified and adapted to the context of large-scale construction project teams. Initial items were modified to ensure that the wording reflected team-level constructs and project-specific practices, producing a preliminary item pool.

Second, 12 semi-structured interviews were conducted with domain experts, comprising eight academics with expertise in project management, organizational behavior, or information systems, and four construction industry practitioners with direct experience in large-scale construction project delivery. The interviews pursued two aims: (1) to develop contextual understanding of how the study constructs manifest in large-scale construction projects; and (2) to solicit feedback on the relevance, accuracy, and readability of the questionnaire items. Based on expert feedback, several items were revised. For example, one procedural meta-knowledge item originally read ``Our team members know the standard procedures for accomplishing most tasks'', which practitioners noted was too generic to capture cross-organizational coordination realities in large-scale projects. The item was revised to ``Our team members know which procedures or channels to use when they need knowledge or skills they do not possess'' (PM-2; Table~3), better reflecting the multi-party knowledge-access complexity inherent in such projects.

Third, a pilot study was conducted to validate the revised questionnaire. A total of 47 responses were collected from project professionals recruited through the research team's networks. For each item, the corrected item-total correlation was calculated, and Cronbach's $\alpha$ was computed for each construct. All corrected item-total correlation values exceeded the recommended threshold of 0.50, and all Cronbach's $\alpha$ values exceeded 0.70, confirming satisfactory item consistency and internal reliability across all constructs.

\subsection{Measures}
Building on the expert interview insights from Section 3.3, all constructs were operationalized using established scales adapted from prior research to fit the large-scale construction project context. To ensure construct equivalence in the Chinese project setting, all measurement items were translated using a back-translation procedure. The translation process involved independent forward translation into Chinese and back-translation into English. Discrepancies were discussed and resolved to ensure conceptual consistency with the original scales while maintaining relevance to large-scale project contexts in China. All items were measured on a five-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (“strongly disagree”) to 5 (“strongly agree”). 

Digital communication tool (DCT) usage was measured using eight items adapted from \citet{chen2020Integrating}, including four items capturing work-oriented use (WU) and four capturing social-oriented use (SU). Structural and relational meta-knowledge were each assessed using three-item scales adapted from \citet{engelbrecht2019How}. Procedural meta-knowledge was measured using three items adapted from \citet{faraj2000Coordinating} and modified to reflect the project context.  Improvisation capability (IC) was measured using seven items from \citet{magni2009Role}, comprising three items for immediacy (IMM) and four for creativity (CRE). All items were adapted to the construction project context and are reported in Table~3.

\begin{center}[Insert Table 3 here]\end{center}

Given that the unit of analysis is the project team, four project-level control variables were included. Project type and project investment size were controlled because different project contexts present varying levels of complexity and coordination demands that may influence both meta-knowledge formation and improvisation capability. Project duration was controlled because longer projects may allow more time for meta-knowledge to develop. Regulatory uncertainty was controlled because projects operating under volatile regulatory environments may face greater external pressures that independently affect team improvisation; it was measured using three items adapted from \citet{wang2016Governing}. \subsection{Analytical methods}
This study employed covariance-based structural equation modeling (CB-SEM) to test the direct-effect hypotheses (H1a--H5b), using the lavaan package in R \citep{rosseel2012Lavaan}. CB-SEM was selected over partial least squares SEM (PLS-SEM) for three reasons. First, this study is theory-driven and aims to confirm a pre-specified theoretical model with directional hypotheses. The confirmatory orientation of CB-SEM is well suited to this purpose \citep{anderson1988Structural, hair2019Multivariate}. Second, all constructs are operationalized using reflective multi-item scales with established psychometric properties, satisfying the common factor model assumptions underlying CB-SEM \citep{pesamaa2021Publishing}. Third, the sample size of 255 comfortably meets the requirements for stable maximum likelihood estimation in CB-SEM. Following the recommended two-step approach \citep{anderson1988Structural}, the measurement model was evaluated via confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) prior to structural estimation.

The hypothesized mediation effects (H6a--H6f) were tested using bias-corrected bootstrap confidence intervals based on 5,000 resamples \citep{preacher2008Asymptotic}. This approach is preferred over the Sobel test because it does not assume normality of the sampling distribution of indirect effects and provides more accurate interval estimates, particularly for small-to-medium samples \citep{preacher2008Asymptotic}. Given that improvisation capability comprises two dimensions (immediacy and creativity), mediation was assessed separately for each outcome.

\subsection{Robustness checks}
The three-wave time-lagged design employed in this study establishes temporal precedence for the proposed causal direction, as independent variables (T1), mediators (T2), and dependent variables (T3) were measured at separate time points with one-month intervals. Combined with the procedural and statistical common method bias controls reported in Section 3.3, these design features substantially mitigate threats to causal interpretation.

To further assess robustness, we examined whether the core findings are sensitive to how improvisation capability is operationalized. The main analysis modeled immediacy (IMM) and creativity (CRE) as two separate first-order dependent variables, following \citet{magni2009Role}. However, prior research has also operationalized improvisation capability as a higher-order construct \citep{charoensukmongkol2019Entrepreneurial}. We therefore re-estimated the structural model with IC specified as a reflective second-order latent variable measured by IMM and CRE, and re-tested all structural paths and mediation effects without control variables. The second-order model demonstrated good fit ($\chi^{2}$(238) = 332.79, CFI = 0.969, TLI = 0.964, RMSEA = 0.040, SRMR = 0.078). As shown in Table 7, the direction and significance of all six IV-to-mediator paths remained consistent with the first-order model:

WU significantly predicted SM ($\beta$ = 0.307, p < 0.001) and PM ($\beta$ = 0.344, p < 0.001) but not RM, while SU significantly predicted SM ($\beta$ = 0.232, p < 0.01) and RM ($\beta$ = 0.237, p < 0.05) but not PM. Overall, these results demonstrate that the core findings are robust to the operationalization of the dependent variable.

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{Discussion on the research questions}

\subsubsection{How digital communication tool use shapes improvisation capability through differentiated pathways (RQ1)}
The empirical results suggest that DCT use shapes improvisation capability through two stages, answering RQ1. First, work-oriented and social-oriented use cultivate distinct forms of meta-knowledge. Second, these forms of meta-knowledge exert different effects on the project team's immediacy and creativity.

Regarding the first stage, the results reveal asymmetric patterns in how different forms of DCT use shape meta-knowledge. Work-oriented use has significant positive effects on structural and procedural meta-knowledge but not on relational meta-knowledge. Social-oriented use enhances structural and relational meta-knowledge but shows no significant effect on procedural meta-knowledge.

These asymmetric meta-knowledge profiles can be explained through the externalization mechanism central to the distributed cognition perspective: DCTs convert transient team interactions into retrievable digital traces, and the type of trace deposited depends on the orientation of use \citep{treem2020ComputerMediated, leonardi2014Social}. Work-oriented use externalizes task cognition into structured cognitive artifacts that encode information about contributors' expertise domains and procedural routines \citep{drury-grogan2021Changes, cho2022Digital}. By accumulating retrievable records of ``who knows what'' and ``how work gets done'', these task-oriented traces provide a rich informational substrate for structural and procedural meta-knowledge \citep{engelbrecht2019How}. However, the traces deposited by work-oriented use carry limited socio-emotional content: they record coordination outcomes but not the interpersonal preferences and collaboration styles from which relational awareness develops \citep{palazzolo2006Coevolution}. Social-oriented use demonstrates a complementary pattern. Rather than depositing task logs, social interactions externalize relational cognition into perceivable digital traces \citep{brockhaus2023Digitalization}. This directly strengthens relational meta-knowledge by making team members' collaboration preferences and interpersonal ties visible \citep{leonardi2015Ambient}. Consistent with \citet{fu2025Enterprise}, the spontaneous and less structured nature of social interaction does not produce the process records from which procedural meta-knowledge develops. Through these complementary externalization modes, work-oriented and social-oriented use jointly construct a distributed cognitive foundation, and their differential effects on relational and procedural meta-knowledge reflect the distinct cognitive content each usage orientation externalizes \citep{hutchins2020Distributed}.

Regarding the second stage, the results show that the three meta-knowledge dimensions do not uniformly contribute to improvisation capability. Structural meta-knowledge is positively associated with both immediacy and creativity. More nuanced patterns emerge for relational and procedural meta-knowledge: relational meta-knowledge shows no significant relationship with creativity, and procedural meta-knowledge does not exert a significant positive effect on immediacy.

A knowledge integration lens helps specify the mechanisms underlying this asymmetry \citep{perry-smith2017Creativity}. Structural meta-knowledge functions as a general-purpose resource that supports both immediacy and creativity by enabling the project team to locate and align relevant expertise regardless of the specific performance demand. Relational and procedural meta-knowledge, in contrast, correspond to more specialized integration pathways. Immediacy relies on the rapid mobilization and alignment of relevant expertise; knowing whom to contact under time pressure allows the project team to bypass formal channels and coordinate immediately \citep{okhuysen200910}. This mechanism is amplified in large-scale construction projects, where multiple organizational tiers impose rigid hierarchical approval processes that are too slow to accommodate emergent disruptions \citep{shen2022Largescale}. In such contexts, relational meta-knowledge enables team members to bypass these rigid cross-organizational hierarchies and activate informal trust networks, securing the rapid response time that immediacy demands \citep{jiang2025Confronting}. Creativity depends more on the recombination of heterogeneous knowledge inputs; the team's shared awareness of work processes provides routines that can be flexibly recombined to generate novel solutions \citep{perry-smith2017Creativity}. The deep cross-disciplinary knowledge heterogeneity in large-scale construction projects intensifies this recombination challenge \citep{yang2025How}. Procedural meta-knowledge serves not merely as a repository of standard routines but as a boundary-spanning protocol that provides experts from disparate disciplines with a shared procedural language, enabling fragmented knowledge to be rapidly recombined \citep{xie2025How}. Our findings corroborate \citet{soda2019Network}, who suggest that coordination-oriented interaction structures tend to streamline communication but may simultaneously reduce exposure to knowledge domains that enable novel recombination. This efficiency--innovation tension explains why relational meta-knowledge supports immediacy but not creativity, and why procedural meta-knowledge supports creativity but not immediacy.

\subsubsection{Meta-knowledge as a selective mediator (RQ2)}
The mediation analysis provides a nuanced answer to RQ2. Meta-knowledge does mediate the relationship between DCT use and improvisation capability, but the mediation is selective rather than comprehensive. Structural meta-knowledge consistently transmits the effects of DCT use to improvisation capability, whereas relational and procedural meta-knowledge operate as mediators only along specific paths.

The consistency of structural meta-knowledge as a mediator can be explained by the affordances of digital communication environments. DCTs generate highly visible traces of communication and problem solving. Such visibility makes expertise signaling and knowledge location salient, enabling project team members to recognize and update their understanding of expertise distribution through routine digital interaction \citep{chen2020Integrating}. This affordance acquires particular significance in large-scale construction projects, where team members face dual fragmentation: physical dispersion across multiple work sites and organizational dispersion across project participants \citep{shen2022Largescale}. As temporary organizations, these project teams lack the shared work history and tacit mutual understanding that accumulate through long-term collaboration \citep{bakker2010Taking, mariano2024Managing}. Without such pre-existing cognitive foundations, the visible digital traces generated by DCTs become the primary and most efficient pathway through which team members establish ``who knows what'', regardless of whether the interaction is task-focused or socially oriented. Structural meta-knowledge therefore emerges as a dual-pathway mediator because it is reliably supplied by both usage orientations, broadly relevant to both dimensions of improvisation capability, and acutely demanded in project contexts where pre-existing cognitive foundations are absent.

The selective mediation of relational and procedural meta-knowledge reveals what we term a \textit{cognitive-capability alignment} mechanism. From a distributed cognition perspective, digital interaction generates abundant but undifferentiated information flows within the cognitive system \citep{hutchins2020Distributed}. A complete mediation path materializes only when three elements align: the interaction cues that a usage orientation externalizes, the cognitive structure (i.e., the meta-knowledge dimension) that these cues develop, and the capability dimension whose resource integration demands match that cognitive structure. Relational meta-knowledge mediates the social-oriented use--immediacy path because social interaction supplies the socio-emotional cues necessary for relational awareness, and relational awareness in turn supports rapid mobilization under time pressure. However, this path does not extend to creativity because relational awareness alone does not provide the procedural repertoire needed for creative recombination. Similarly, procedural meta-knowledge mediates the work-oriented use--creativity path because task-focused interaction externalizes work processes, and the team's procedural awareness supports novel recombination. This path does not extend to immediacy because procedural templates alone are insufficient for immediate alignment under urgency \citep{ren2011Transactive, marion2021Transformation}.

These findings indicate that the cognitive consequences of digital interaction are structured and uneven. Digital communication generates abundant interaction, but only some of this interaction crystallizes into team-level cognitive resources that connect specific usage patterns to specific capability outcomes \citep{ye2021Leveraging, zaverzhenets2021Digitalising}. The cognitive-capability alignment mechanism suggests that prior research reporting inconsistent or contradictory effects of digital collaboration \citep{kane2014Whats} may have overlooked this alignment logic: without differentiating among interaction orientations, meta-knowledge dimensions, and capability demands, the selective nature of mediation remains masked behind aggregate effects. The relationship between digital collaboration and team capability is therefore more contingent than often assumed, governed by the degree of alignment across the externalization--cognition--capability chain. \subsection{Theoretical implications}

This study offers four theoretical implications for the project management and knowledge management literature. First, it extends the project improvisation literature by identifying digital communication tool use as a distinct, technology-based antecedent. By establishing that work-oriented and social-oriented usage cultivate distinct forms of meta-knowledge that selectively enable immediacy and creativity, this study introduces a technological and cognitive pathway. It demonstrates that in the highly fragmented environment of large-scale construction projects, communication technology is not merely a backdrop but an active enabler that converts distributed expertise into rapid improvisational action.

Second, this study reframes digital communication in project research by showing that digital communication tools can build team cognitive infrastructure rather than merely improving communication efficiency. The findings indicate that digital communication does not produce uniform effects; instead, its consequences depend on how digital tools are used within project teams. Digital communication therefore operates as a system through which project teams develop the cognitive awareness that enables coordinated action. By shifting attention from technology adoption to interaction orientation, this study positions digital communication as a cognitive infrastructure-building process that underpins team capability in complex project environments \citep{leonardi2015Ambient}.

Third, this study contributes to the project management literature by introducing a three-dimensional meta-knowledge framework into a context where it has been largely absent. Project management research, in contrast, has predominantly examined knowledge itself while paying limited attention to the higher-order awareness of how knowledge is distributed and mobilized. The findings demonstrate that structural, relational, and procedural meta-knowledge each play distinct roles in shaping improvisation capability. These differentiated effects reveal that meta-knowledge is not a monolithic cognitive resource but a multifaceted structure whose dimensions connect to team capability through different pathways. The study thus extends meta-knowledge theory beyond its traditional organizational boundary into temporary, cross-organizational project settings.

Fourth, the findings extend the application of the distributed cognition perspective by showing that team capability depends not only on knowledge possession but also on the ability to mobilize distributed knowledge. Traditional project research has emphasized knowledge acquisition, expertise, and learning as primary drivers of performance, implicitly assuming that teams perform better when members possess more knowledge or higher levels of expertise. The present study shows that, in digitally mediated project environments, the ability to locate, combine, and mobilize distributed knowledge is as important as knowledge possession and can be even more critical under time pressure. Project team improvisation capability therefore depends on the team's collective awareness of how knowledge is distributed within the project team, how it can be accessed, and how it is enacted in practice.

\subsection{Practical implications}
This study provides practical insights for project managers seeking to leverage digital communication tools to support improvisation capability. First, the findings reveal that immediacy and creativity follow distinct meta-knowledge pathways. Project managers should therefore conduct differentiated capability assessments before designing team development strategies. This assessment involves two dimensions. At the project-type level, managers should identify which form of improvisation capability their project demands most urgently. Projects characterized by high safety risks, tight schedules, and frequent unexpected disruptions, such as urban tunnel shield boring, place a premium on immediacy. In such contexts, strengthening social-oriented use to cultivate relational meta-knowledge is essential, as knowing whom to contact and how to reach them enables rapid coordinated action. Conversely, projects involving technically complex solutions and multi-disciplinary interfaces, such as complex foundation engineering, demand creativity. Here, reinforcing work-oriented use to develop procedural meta-knowledge is more effective, as the team's shared awareness of standard workflows and approval procedures provides the structured foundation upon which creative recombination can occur. At the project lifecycle level, immediacy needs peak during construction surges and final commissioning phases when disruptions cluster, while creativity needs are highest during early planning, detailed design, and value engineering stages.

Second, project managers should treat digital communication tool usage as a knowledge-building process rather than merely a communication channel. The findings indicate that no single form of digital interaction generates the full range of meta-knowledge required for improvisation. Project managers should therefore design digital collaboration environments that intentionally combine both task-focused coordination and informal interaction spaces. Modern digital communication platforms such as DingTalk, Feishu, and Microsoft Teams now offer low-code customization capabilities that project teams can leverage to build meta-knowledge-oriented workspaces. For example, teams can create expertise directory pages that tag members by specialty, certification, and prior project experience (supporting structural meta-knowledge), organizational relationship dashboards that visualize cross-unit liaison contacts and collaboration history (supporting relational meta-knowledge), and standardized workflow libraries that catalogue construction procedures and approval sequences (supporting procedural meta-knowledge). Automated onboarding workflows can further support meta-knowledge accumulation by automatically pushing expert directories to new team members, thereby reducing the time needed to develop an adequate understanding of the team's distributed knowledge landscape.

Third, project managers should build project-level distributed cognition systems that use digital communication tools as the hub connecting dispersed knowledge carriers into a searchable and retrievable cognitive infrastructure. Distributed cognition theory posits that cognition resides not solely within individual minds but across the network of people, tools, and artifacts that constitute the working environment. Applying this principle, project managers can design systems whose components map directly onto the three meta-knowledge dimensions identified in this study, as illustrated in Fig. 4. For structural meta-knowledge, the system should maintain an expert capability atlas, a searchable repository documenting each participating organization's specializations, professional qualifications, key personnel, and relevant project experience, so that team members can rapidly locate the right expertise when unexpected problems arise. For relational meta-knowledge, the system should capture the collaboration network, including cross-organizational liaison contacts, historical cooperation records, and communication frequency logs, enabling teams to identify trusted coordination channels under time pressure. For procedural meta-knowledge, the system should house a procedure knowledge base containing standardized construction sequences, emergency response plans, construction method templates, variation approval workflows, and relevant regulatory policies. Beyond these three pillars, the system should incorporate project decision logs that record the rationale behind key technical choices, a lessons-learned repository that archives post-incident reviews and project retrospectives, and real-time alert mechanisms that push critical updates to relevant personnel when conditions change.

\subsection{Limitations and future research directions}
This study has several limitations that should be acknowledged. First, the proposed meta-knowledge pathways were examined within the context of large-scale construction projects. These features make meta-knowledge critical for coordination and improvisation. However, the prominence of meta-knowledge observed here may be partly context-specific. In projects with more stable membership, higher task modularity, or stronger technological integration, such as software development or R\&D teams, DCT use may generate different cognitive consequences. Future research should examine how project characteristics shape the relative importance of different meta-knowledge pathways and explore the boundary conditions of the proposed framework across diverse project settings.

Second, all team-level constructs were measured using a single key informant per project team. While this approach is well established in organizational research and is appropriate when informants have comprehensive knowledge of team processes, it cannot directly verify whether other team members would report similar perceptions. Future research could collect data from multiple members per team to examine within-team consensus and strengthen the validity of team-level construct measurement.

Third, the empirical data were collected exclusively from large-scale construction project teams in China. China's construction industry exhibits distinctive institutional and cultural characteristics that may shape how DCT use influences meta-knowledge development. At the same time, the theoretical mechanisms underpinning the proposed model, specifically the externalization of task and relational cognition through DCT use and the resulting development of team-level meta-knowledge, are grounded in distributed cognition principles that are not inherently tied to a particular national context. Large-scale construction projects in other countries similarly involve dispersed expertise, cross-organizational boundaries, and time-compressed coordination demands, suggesting that the core pathways identified in this study are likely to extend beyond the Chinese context. However, caution is warranted when generalizing the findings to other national contexts, particularly those with different digital tool ecosystems and contrasting cultural orientations toward knowledge sharing. Future research should examine whether these pathways replicate in different institutional and cultural settings.

\section{Conclusion}
This study examines how digital communication tool use shapes improvisation capability in large-scale construction project teams through meta-knowledge development. Drawing on distributed cognition theory, the study develops and tests a theoretical model that distinguishes work-oriented and social-oriented DCT use and conceptualizes structural, relational, and procedural meta-knowledge as cognitive mechanisms linking digital interaction to team capability.

The conclusions are twofold. First, work-oriented and social-oriented DCT use shape improvisation capability through differentiated meta-knowledge pathways rather than uniform effects. Work-oriented use cultivates structural and procedural meta-knowledge, whereas social-oriented use cultivates structural and relational meta-knowledge, indicating that interaction orientation determines which cognitive resources are developed. Second, meta-knowledge mediates the DCT use--improvisation capability relationship selectively rather than comprehensively. Structural meta-knowledge consistently transmits the effects of both usage orientations to both dimensions of improvisation capability, while relational and procedural meta-knowledge function as mediators only along specific paths. Together, these findings demonstrate that the influence of DCT use on team capability depends on how digital interaction reshapes team cognitive infrastructure, and that this reshaping varies systematically with usage orientation.

These findings contribute to research on digital collaboration, distributed cognition, and project teamwork by clarifying the cognitive mechanisms through which digital interaction translates into team capability. They also offer guidance for project organizations seeking to design digital collaboration practices that support both rapid response and creative problem-solving in complex project environments. Because the empirical context is limited to large-scale construction project teams in China, caution is warranted when generalizing these results to other project types and national contexts.
