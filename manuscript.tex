\begin{abstract}
Improvisation capability enables project teams to respond effectively to unexpected situations, yet how digital communication tool use shapes this capability remains underexplored. This study develops and tests a model linking work-oriented and social-oriented digital communication tool use to improvisation capability through meta-knowledge---shared awareness of expertise, relationships, and procedures. Three-wave time-lagged survey data from 255 project members in China show that different usage patterns foster complementary forms of meta-knowledge, which selectively transmit effects to improvisation capability. Structural meta-knowledge emerges as the dominant mediator, whereas relational and procedural meta-knowledge exhibit more selective effects. This research contributes by distinguishing work-oriented from social-oriented tool use, introducing procedural meta-knowledge as a novel construct, and identifying differentiated pathways linking digital collaboration to improvisation capability. The findings offer practical guidance for designing digital collaboration practices that enhance team capability in uncertain project environments.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Large-scale construction projects, characterized by long cycles, complex technical interdependencies, and multiple-stakeholder involvement \citep{yang2025How,mariano2024Managing}, often encounter uncertainties \citep{shen2022Largescale}. Traditional plan-driven project management approaches are challenged by the need for quick responses to contingencies \citep{malucelli2021Facing,coulon2026Wing}. Project team members' improvisation has emerged as a critical ability for addressing these challenges \citep{jiang2025Confronting,sumbal2025Improvisation}. \textbf{\textit{Improvisation capability}} refers to the ability to spontaneously create and implement solutions to unexpected project contingencies by rapidly mobilizing and recombining existing knowledge and resources \citep{magni2009Role,veranghg2005Improvisation}. For instance, the final joint of the Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macao Bridge immersed tunnel exceeded its acceptance tolerance. In response, the project members improvised an unprecedented reverse-operation to re-dock the structure, reducing the deviation from 17 centimeters to 2.5 millimeters within 38 hours \citep{21cbh2018Redocking}. The convergence of planning and execution in real time and the generation of novel responses distinguish improvisation capability from routine problem-solving \citep{moorman1998Organizational, miner2001Organizational}. Enhancing project team members' improvisation capability is of practical importance, especially given the difficulty of comprehensive planning in a temporary organization setting \citep{wang2025Formalization,jiang2023Toward}.

Existing project management research on antecedents of project members' improvisation capability has concentrated on three areas, as shown in Table 1. First, cognitive and psychological states, such as collective mindfulness \citep{jiang2025Confronting}, psychological safety \citep{huang2026Thriving}, and facilitating conditions \citep{coulon2026Wing}, have been shown to enable improvisation capability. Second, the knowledge and experience base, including tacit knowledge and sensemaking \citep{sumbal2025Improvisation} and ambidextrous search \citep{rui2022Contingent, klein2015Improvisation}, contributes to improvisation capability development. Third, contextual and environmental antecedents, such as project complexity \citep{mamedio2020Managing, jiang2023Toward}, plan deviations \citep{vuorinen2019Lifecycle}, and uncertainty \citep{hacker2019Legitimizing}, prompt improvisational responses. Yet, limited study has examined the role of communication technology through which knowledge is shared and mobilized.

Effective improvisation depends on the real-time mobilization of distributed knowledge \citep{magni2009Role, moorman1998Organizational}. Compared with general projects, large-scale construction projects involve a far greater number of participating organizations \citep{hu2024Performance}, deeper cross-disciplinary knowledge heterogeneity \citep{yang2025How}, and wider geographical dispersion of work sites \citep{shen2022Largescale}. These characteristics amplify the challenge of real-time knowledge mobilization. When unexpected events arise, conventional communication mechanisms, such as scheduled meetings and formal documentation, cannot operate at the speed that improvisation demands. \textbf{\textit{Digital communication tools (DCTs)}} are well positioned to address this problem. As the primary medium to coordinate and exchange expertise in real time, DCTs enable team members to instantly reach across organizational and geographical boundaries to locate relevant knowledge and mobilize it for immediate action \citep{zhang2018Mobile}. In practice, mobile social media platforms such as WhatsApp, Microsoft Teams, WeChat, and Slack have become essential for cross-organizational coordination, mediating task assignment, technical problem-solving, and informal exchange \citep{ma2022Investigating, jia2025Knowledge}. However, the project management literature has not examined whether and how DCT use contributes to project members' improvisation capability. 

Moreover, DCT use in construction project teams is not a homogeneous behavior. In practice, team members use DCTs both for task-focused coordination, such as assigning work, solving technical problems, and tracking progress, and for relationship-oriented exchange, such as sharing experiences, seeking informal advice, and maintaining interpersonal connections. Prior research in the construction project context has confirmed this distinction, showing that work-oriented and social-oriented use patterns produce different effects on project outcomes \citep{ma2022Understanding, jiang2026Bolstering}. Yet how these distinct usage patterns differentially contribute to improvisation capability remains unexplored. \textbf{\textit{Therefore, the first aim of this study is to examine how work-oriented and social-oriented DCT use differentially influence improvisation capability in large-scale construction projects.}}

A critical problem, then, is through what cognitive mechanism these distinct DCT use patterns translate into improvisation capability. In large-scale construction projects, DCTs generate a substantial volume of information flows across organizational boundaries \citep{treem2013Social}. These flows increase the knowledge density accessible to each team member \citep{leonardi2015Ambient}. Improvisation demands that relevant expertise be located and mobilized within extremely compressed time frames. Without shared awareness of who holds what knowledge and how it can be accessed, team members risk being overwhelmed by abundant but undirected information \citep{sun2021Dark}. They cannot convert the knowledge available through DCTs into timely improvised action. This cognitive resource---the shared awareness of knowledge distribution and access within the team---is what the literature terms \textbf{\textit{meta-knowledge}} \citep{wegner1987Transactive, leonardi2014Social}. Yet, the project management literature has predominantly examined knowledge itself, focusing on knowledge sharing, knowledge transfer, and knowledge integration as enablers of project performance \citep{jia2025Knowledge, hu2024Performance}, while paying limited attention to meta-knowledge. Without understanding how team members develop shared awareness of knowledge distribution and access, the field cannot explain the cognitive foundation of improvised action in large-scale construction projects. Research in information systems has demonstrated that enterprise social media can enhance meta-knowledge through communication visibility \citep{leonardi2014Social, engelbrecht2019How}. Yet the project management field has not examined how DCT use cultivates this cognitive resource, nor how different usage patterns may do so through distinct pathways. \textbf{\textit{Therefore, the second aim of this study is to examine how meta-knowledge serves as the bridging cognitive mechanism through which work-oriented and social-oriented DCT use influence improvisation capability.}}

This study examines how work-oriented and social-oriented DCT use influence improvisation capability through the development of meta-knowledge in large-scale construction projects. We integrate the characteristics of large-scale construction project teams (highly dispersed expertise, limited shared work history, and time-compressed coordination) with the meta-knowledge literature \citep{wegner1987Transactive, leonardi2014Social}. On this basis, we examine three dimensions of team members' meta-knowledge: structural meta-knowledge (knowing who knows what), relational meta-knowledge (knowing who knows whom), and procedural meta-knowledge (knowing how to access and mobilize knowledge). We test the proposed model using three-wave time-lagged survey data collected from 255 professionals involved in large infrastructure projects in China. This study draws on distributed cognition perspective \citep{hutchins2020Distributed}, which conceptualizes cognition as emerging from interactions among individuals, technological artifacts, and the social environment. This theoretical lens is particularly suited to our context because it treats DCTs not merely as passive channels but as active constituents of the cognitive system through which meta-knowledge is developed and improvisation capability is enacted \citep{leonardi2015Ambient}. Accordingly, this study addresses the following research questions (RQs):

\textbf{RQ1:} \textit{How do work-oriented and social-oriented digital communication tool use influence improvisation capability in large-scale construction projects?}

\textbf{RQ2:} \textit{Does meta-knowledge mediate the relationship between digital communication tool use and improvisation capability?}

The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. Section 2 presents the theoretical foundations and develops the research hypotheses. Section 3 describes the methodology. Section 4 presents the results. Section 5 discusses the findings and their implications. Section 6 concludes with limitations and directions for future research.

\section{Theoretical foundations and hypotheses development}
\subsection{Improvisation capability in large-scale construction projects}
The concept of improvisation originated from analogies between jazz performance and organizational action, with early work defining improvisation primarily as the convergence of planning and execution \citep{moorman1998Organizational}. Subsequent scholarship further reconceptualized improvisation as a process in which existing resources and knowledge are creatively recombined to produce novel responses under constraints \citep{miner2001Organizational, crossan2003Making}. Integrating both the temporal and knowledge-recombination perspectives, \citet{veranghg2005Improvisation} and \citet{magni2009Role} operationalized improvisation capability as comprising two dimensions: \textbf{\textit{immediacy and creativity}}. \textbf{\textit{Immediacy}} captures the capacity to formulate and implement solutions with minimal delay between conception and action, reflecting an individual's responsiveness under time pressure \citep{veranghg2005Improvisation}. \textbf{\textit{Creativity}} reflects the ability to generate novel solutions through non-routine recombination of available knowledge and resources, producing outcomes that go beyond established procedures \citep{magni2009Role}. This two-dimensional conceptualization suggests that effective improvisation requires not merely speed of response, but also the quality and originality of the solution produced under temporal constraints. 

In the project management domain, improvisation capability has received growing scholarly attention, particularly in large-scale construction projects where technical complexity, cross-organizational workforce composition, and geographical dispersion of work sites amplify both the frequency of unexpected contingencies and the difficulty of improvised response \citep{klein2015Improvisation, malucelli2021Facing, jiang2023Toward}. Table~\ref{tab:antecedents} synthesizes the existing research on antecedents of improvisation capability in the project management literature, suggesting three categories. First, cognitive and psychological states, demonstrate that conditions such as collective mindfulness \citep{jiang2025Confronting, wang2025Formalization}, psychological safety \citep{huang2026Thriving}, and facilitating conditions \citep{coulon2026Wing} create a state of readiness that enables spontaneous action. Second, knowledge and experience base, establishes that improvisation draws on existing knowledge stocks, including tacit knowledge \citep{sumbal2025Improvisation}, bricolage \citep{xie2025How}, ambidextrous search \citep{rui2022Contingent}, and theoretical knowledge foundations \citep{klein2015Improvisation, nisula2016Role}. Third, contextual and environmental triggers, identify conditions such as project complexity \citep{mamedio2020Managing, jiang2023Toward}, plan deviations \citep{hamzeh2018Investigating, vuorinen2019Lifecycle}, and extreme uncertainty \citep{hacker2019Legitimizing, malucelli2021Facing} that necessitate improvisation. 

Despite these contributions, all three streams focus on the preconditions of improvisation (readiness, knowledge stocks, and triggering contexts), without examining the infrastructure through which distributed knowledge is located and mobilized during improvised action. Psychological readiness does not automatically translate into coordinated action across organizational boundaries. Possessing knowledge is not equivalent to mobilizing it under temporal constraints. None of these streams examines the communication tools through which project team members identify who holds relevant expertise, gain access to it, and recombine it for immediate action. The absence of communication technology from existing antecedent research is notable given the centrality of DCTs in contemporary project practice. In large-scale construction projects, DCTs have become the primary medium through which team members coordinate tasks, share technical knowledge, and reach across organizational boundaries in real time \citep{zhang2018Mobile, jia2025Knowledge}, constituting the primary infrastructure for cross-boundary knowledge mobilization \citep{ma2022Investigating}. A systematic examination of how DCT use shapes improvisation capability is therefore warranted.

\subsection{Digital communication tool use in project teams}
DCT research in organizational settings has established that visibility, persistence, editability, and association of DCTs can transform communication from ephemeral and dyadic exchanges into persistent and collectively visible traces \citep{treem2013Social}. These characteristics create ambient awareness of distributed activities and expertise \citep{leonardi2014Social}. In project contexts, a growing body of empirical work demonstrates the centrality of DCTs. \citet{zhang2018Mobile} showed that mobile social media platforms such as WeChat and WhatsApp serve as primary tools for cross-organizational virtual collaboration in inter-organizational projects. \citet{jia2025Knowledge} found that mobile social media groups have become the dominant channel for knowledge sharing within construction project teams, while \citet{ma2022Investigating} demonstrated that DCT-mediated communication facilitates cross-boundary coordination and collaborative problem-solving in construction projects. These studies establish that DCTs are deeply embedded in the daily work of construction project teams, yet research in this area has focused predominantly on coordination and performance outcomes of DCT use without differentiating how DCT use shapes individual-level cognitive resources.

Research on technology use has increasingly distinguished between work-oriented and social-oriented patterns of engagement based on the purposes that guide user behavior \citep{ali-hassan2015Linking, cao2019Exploring}. Work-oriented use refers to purposeful, task-driven interactions such as searching for technical information, assigning tasks, discussing project problems, and coordinating schedules \citep{ma2022Investigating}. Social-oriented use refers to relationship-driven interactions such as sharing personal experiences, seeking informal advice, exchanging emotional support, and maintaining interpersonal connections \citep{ali-hassan2015Linking, huang2017Ties}. In construction project settings, these two patterns produce distinct effects: work-oriented use primarily enhances task performance, while social-oriented use strengthens relational coordination \citep{ma2022Understanding, jiang2026Bolstering}. These differential effects are likely amplified in large-scale construction projects, where more pronounced cross-organizational boundaries and wider geographical dispersion heighten the need for both task coordination and relationship maintenance. More recently, \citet{zhu2024Effects} demonstrated that enterprise social media use can enhance employees' improvisation ability through psychological mechanisms, providing initial evidence that DCT use is relevant to improvisation. However, how work-oriented and social-oriented use patterns differentially influence the cognitive resources that underpin improvisation capability in project teams, specifically meta-knowledge, remains unexplored.

\subsection{Meta-knowledge in project teams}
Meta-knowledge refers to an individual's awareness of how knowledge and expertise are distributed, connected, and mobilized within a collective \citep{wegner1987Transactive}. In the transactive memory systems literature, meta-knowledge originated as the cognitive component that enables team members to locate relevant expertise without possessing it themselves \citep{lewis2003Measuring, kush2019Conceptual}. 

Three research streams contribute to the present understanding of meta-knowledge. First, transactive memory systems research established that effective team coordination depends on members' awareness of how expertise is distributed across the team \citep{wegner1987Transactive, lewis2003Measuring}. This dimension refers to "knowing which team members hold which domain-specific knowledge" \citep{kush2019Conceptual, ellwart2014Team}. We term this content-based awareness \textbf{\textit{structural meta-knowledge (SM)}}: an individual's awareness of how expertise and knowledge domains are distributed across team members, enabling efficient identification of appropriate knowledge sources. 

Second, social network and communication visibility research has demonstrated that individuals also develop awareness of the relational pathways through which knowledge can be accessed \citep{borgatti2003Relational, leonardi2014Social}. Studies of enterprise social media have shown that communication visibility enables individuals to perceive not only who knows what but also who knows whom, identifying the interpersonal ties and intermediaries through which expertise can be reached \citep{fu2025Enterprise, engelbrecht2019How}. We term this pathway-based awareness \textbf{\textit{relational meta-knowledge (RM)}}: an individual's awareness of social ties and interaction pathways through which distributed knowledge can be accessed. 

Third, team coordination research has highlighted that team members also develop awareness of how distributed expertise is typically mobilized and combined in practice \citep{faraj2000Coordinating}. The coordination dimension of TMS \citep{lewis2003Measuring, kush2019Conceptual}, interaction molding routines in complex project work \citep{korotkova2024Do}, and meta-knowledge of organizational processes \citep{barmeyer2019Informal} all point to a process-oriented facet of meta-knowledge. We term this action-oriented awareness \textbf{\textit{procedural meta-knowledge (PM)}}: an individual's awareness of how distributed expertise is typically mobilized, combined, and enacted within the team, including cross-disciplinary problem-solving routines and established approaches for responding to recurring challenges.

Synthesizing these three streams, this study adopts a three-dimensional conceptualization of meta-knowledge encompassing SM, RM, and PM. Empirical meta-knowledge work has been conducted almost exclusively within stable organizational teams, such as R\&D groups, management teams, and entrepreneurial ventures \citep{mohammadparsttabas2024Igniting, ren2011Transactive, kush2019Conceptual}, where membership continuity allows knowledge structures to gradually stabilize. Research situated in project management contexts, particularly large-scale construction projects, remains scarce. Large-scale project teams face conditions that make meta-knowledge particularly important yet difficult to develop \citep{korotkova2024Do}. The present study thus proposes that meta-knowledge serves as the mediating mechanism through which DCT use influences improvisation capability.

\subsection{Distributed cognition perspective}
The distributed cognition perspective provides the theoretical foundation for this study. Originating from Hutchins' studies of navigation teams, this perspective advances three core propositions \citep{hutchins2020Distributed, hollan2000Distributed}. First, cognitive processes are not confined to individual minds but are distributed across individuals, artifacts, and the environment. Second, technological artifacts are not passive conduits for information but active participants in cognitive processes, shaping how information is represented, stored, and retrieved. Third, the appropriate unit of analysis for understanding cognition is the functional system---the ensemble of people, tools, and representations engaged in a task---rather than the isolated individual. Recent applications have extended distributed cognition beyond its original navigational settings to technology-mediated teamwork. Studies have examined how digital artifacts structure and redistribute cognitive processes in design teams \citep{mangalaraj2014Distributed, nemeth2004Using} and how team cognition co-evolves with cognitive artifact use in project management \citep{drury-grogan2021Changes}. These propositions are particularly relevant to large-scale construction project teams, where knowledge is dispersed across organizational boundaries and DCTs serve as the primary artifacts through which coordination and knowledge mobilization occur \citep{leonardi2015Ambient, safadi2024Balancing}. By treating DCTs as constitutive elements of the cognitive system rather than mere communication channels, the distributed cognition perspective offers a theoretical basis for examining how different patterns of tool use reshape the cognitive resources, specifically meta-knowledge, available to team members.

Within this framework, meta-knowledge functions as a form of cognitive indexing within the distributed system, enabling individuals to navigate the knowledge landscape without possessing all relevant expertise themselves \citep{engelbrecht2019How, ye2021Leveraging}. As DCTs become embedded in project work, interaction processes that were previously transient or implicit are externalized through persistent communication traces, visible interaction histories, and routinized collaboration structures \citep{leonardi2014Social, treem2020ComputerMediated}. These externalized traces constitute the informational foundation from which meta-knowledge can develop and be maintained \citep{palazzolo2006Coevolution}. Research suggests that the development of meta-knowledge is closely linked to improvisational action: when team members possess well-developed meta-knowledge, they can rapidly identify, access, and recombine distributed expertise to formulate improvised responses \citep{huang2017Ties, oostervink2016Knowledge}.

\subsection{Hypotheses development}
Within the distributed cognition framework, DCTs function as cognitive artifacts that externalize transient interactions into visible traces \citep{treem2013Social, leonardi2014Social}. Different patterns of tool use generate different types of cognitive traces, supplying the distributed system with distinct foundations for meta-knowledge development. We distinguish two complementary externalization modes: work-oriented use externalizes task cognition, while social-oriented use externalizes relational cognition. In large-scale construction projects, cross-organizational workforce composition and multi-site dispersion prevent members from directly observing each other's expertise and work patterns \citep{yang2025How, shen2022Largescale}, making these DCT-mediated cognitive traces the primary foundation for meta-knowledge formation.

\subsubsection{Work-oriented DCT use and meta-knowledge}
Work-oriented use externalizes task-related contributions into accessible cognitive traces, including technical discussions, problem-solving records, and project coordination logs \citep{hadida2015Use, jiang2013Should}. These traces encode content-based information about contributors' expertise domains \citep{lewis2003Measuring}. In large-scale construction projects, members distributed across multiple organizations and work sites cannot directly observe each other's competence through co-located interaction. The cognitive traces externalized through work-oriented use become the primary window into cross-boundary expertise distribution. Through both task-focused search and ambient exposure to accumulated work records \citep{leonardi2015Ambient}, team members can observe what types of expertise different contributors demonstrate and map substantive knowledge domains to specific actors. This content-based expertise mapping supports the development of SM. Accordingly, we propose:

H1a: Work-oriented use is positively associated with SM.

Work-oriented use also makes task interaction patterns visible through traceable digital records: who responds to whom, how requests are routed, and which actors repeatedly coordinate across tasks \citep{borgatti2003Relational, leonardi2017Social}. These externalized interaction patterns reveal the actual coordination relationships that emerge from task execution, enabling team members to infer interpersonal pathways for accessing knowledge beyond what formal organizational charts prescribe \citep{ding2019Enterprise, leonardi2015Ambient}. In large-scale construction projects where formal authority across organizational boundaries provides limited guidance for knowledge access \citep{shen2022Largescale}, the coordination relationships made apparent through work-oriented use become a window into the relational landscape of the project team. By observing recurring patterns, project members can develop awareness of which interpersonal ties serve as effective channels for reaching distributed expertise. Accordingly, we propose:

H1b: Work-oriented use is positively associated with RM.

Work-oriented use externalizes work processes into searchable cognitive traces that constitute what the distributed cognition perspective terms externalized cognitive representations \citep{anand1998Organizational, faraj2000Coordinating}. Task sequence records, coordination histories, and problem-resolution trajectories accumulate as reviewable, searchable artifacts within the distributed cognitive system. In large-scale construction projects, formal process documentation is often incomplete or fragmented across organizational boundaries \citep{yang2025How}, as participating entities maintain different procedural standards and documentation practices. The task execution traces externalized through work-oriented use serve as a de facto procedural cognitive foundation, allowing team members to observe how similar tasks were previously processed, how coordination sequences unfolded, and how problems were resolved across disciplinary boundaries. By reviewing these accumulated process traces, individuals develop awareness of procedural patterns and action sequences without relying on prolonged shared experience. Accordingly, we propose:

H1c: Work-oriented use is positively associated with PM.

\subsubsection{Social-oriented DCT use and meta-knowledge}
Complementary to the task-cognition externalization of work-oriented use, social-oriented use externalizes social interactions into perceivable relational traces via DCTs \citep{ali-hassan2015Linking, cao2019Exploring}, supplying the distributed cognitive system with relational and experiential information that work-oriented use cannot generate.

Through social interactions, team members gain exposure to colleagues’ personal backgrounds, informal expertise developed outside current project tasks, and tacit knowledge that remains invisible in formal work exchanges \citep{ma2022Investigating}. Unlike the task-performance signals externalized by work-oriented use, social exchanges reveal experiential and contextual information about competence domains that extend beyond formally assigned roles. In large-scale construction projects, cross-organizational boundaries create information silos about expertise distribution \citep{shen2022Largescale}; social-oriented use provides a complementary channel through which members can discover expertise that would otherwise remain hidden within organizational boundaries. As these social cues accumulate, they enrich and refine members’ awareness of how expertise is distributed across the team, complementing the content-based mapping derived from work-oriented use. Accordingly, we propose:

H2a: Social-oriented use is positively associated with SM.

Social-oriented use externalizes relational structures into perceivable interaction records through informal exchanges \citep{huang2017Ties}. Co-engagement patterns, trust-based ties, and intermediary connections become discernible as social interactions accumulate within DCTs. The distributed cognition perspective holds that the cumulative and traceable nature of DCT-mediated interaction converts transient social exchanges into traceable relational information \citep{leonardi2014Social}. Unlike the formal coordination patterns revealed by work-oriented use, these traces reflect trust-based relationships and personal connections that operate outside established reporting structures. In large-scale construction projects, formal authority structures spanning multiple participating organizations provide limited guidance for cross-boundary knowledge access \citep{shen2022Largescale}. Social-oriented use enables members to discover and map informal relational pathways, identifying which interpersonal ties serve as effective channels for reaching distributed expertise. Accordingly, we propose:

H2b: Social-oriented use is positively associated with RM.

Social-oriented use also externalizes procedural cognition through experience sharing, practical advice, and situational discussion \citep{ali-hassan2015Linking, cao2019Exploring}. These social exchanges provide narrative and judgment-based insights into how work is actually carried out in practice, including tacit norms, preferred approaches to handling recurring issues, and situational judgments about when procedural deviations are acceptable \citep{barmeyer2019Informal}. From a distributed cognition perspective, these informally externalized procedural cues complement the explicit process traces generated by work-oriented use: whereas work-oriented use captures sequential task execution patterns, social-oriented use reveals the informal rules of thumb, workarounds, and contextual adaptations that practitioners apply when formal procedures encounter practical constraints. In large-scale construction projects, formal procedural documentation is often fragmented across participating organizations, and standardized protocols may not fully capture the cross-organizational coordination practices that emerge in actual project delivery. The procedural insights externalized through social-oriented use fill these gaps, helping team members develop awareness of how distributed work processes operate beyond what formal documentation prescribes. Accordingly, we propose:

H2c: Social-oriented use is positively associated with PM.

\subsubsection{Meta-knowledge and improvisation capability}
SM, RM, and PM function as different levels of cognitive indexing that enable individuals to navigate the distributed knowledge landscape without possessing all relevant expertise themselves \citep{engelbrecht2019How, ye2021Leveraging}. Improvisation demands that distributed knowledge be mobilized within compressed timeframes to produce effective responses to unexpected situations \citep{magni2009Role}. The cognitive indexing provided by these three dimensions of meta-knowledge determine both the speed with which relevant knowledge can be located and mobilized, and the breadth of knowledge resources available for recombination. These two mobilization characteristics correspond to the two dimensions of improvisation capability: immediacy and creativity \citep{veranghg2005Improvisation}.

SM enables individuals to identify who possesses relevant domain-specific expertise when unexpected problems arise. In large-scale construction projects, expertise distribution spans multiple organizations and work sites, and individuals’ own knowledge is often insufficient to address emergent cross-disciplinary issues. By clarifying the content-based distribution of expertise across team members, SM reduces the cognitive search costs required to locate appropriate knowledge holders \citep{lewis2003Measuring}, enabling rapid identification and mobilization of relevant expertise under time pressure.

SM also facilitates creative responses by allowing individuals to mobilize diverse knowledge domains beyond their immediate task scope \citep{ye2021Leveraging}. In large-scale construction projects, the heterogeneous expertise distributed across disciplinary and organizational boundaries constitutes a rich pool for knowledge recombination. When team members possess awareness of this expertise distribution, they can identify and integrate knowledge elements from different domains, generating solutions that transcend the boundaries of any single discipline. Accordingly, we propose:

H3a: SM is positively associated with immediacy.

H3b: SM is positively associated with creativity.

RM provides awareness of who is connected to whom, which actors serve as intermediaries, and how indirect ties can be activated in practice \citep{huang2017Ties}. This pathway-based awareness shortens the gap between problem recognition and coordinated action \citep{faraj2000Coordinating}. In large-scale construction projects where formal authority across organizational boundaries is limited, RM enables individuals to bypass formal channels and activate interpersonal pathways to reach relevant expertise rapidly.

RM also supports creative responses by facilitating targeted access to knowledge holders across professional boundaries \citep{faraj2000Coordinating}. From a distributed cognition perspective, awareness of interpersonal pathways allows individuals to selectively activate specific relational ties to reach expertise holders in different disciplinary domains \citep{perry-smith2017Creativity, soda2019Network}. This targeted cross-boundary interaction exposes individuals to different professional logics and knowledge elements outside their own disciplinary repertoire. In large-scale construction projects, RM provides the pathway awareness necessary for individuals to reach across organizational and disciplinary boundaries, supporting the recombination of heterogeneous inputs into novel action plans. Accordingly, we propose:

H4a: RM is positively associated with immediacy.

H4b: RM is positively associated with creativity.

PM provides familiar action templates that allow individuals to translate situational cues into action without prolonged deliberation \citep{rulke2000Investigating, turner2022Improving}. From a distributed cognition perspective, PM reduces uncertainty about ``how to act'' by making established action patterns cognitively available as ready-made frameworks for rapid deployment \citep{faraj2000Coordinating}. In emergent situations, delays often stem from uncertainty about how to proceed rather than from a lack of expertise \citep{crossan2003Making}. PM addresses this uncertainty by providing ready-made procedural frameworks, bypassing the need to construct action plans from scratch. In large-scale construction projects, coordination complexity amplifies procedural uncertainty. Team members with well-developed procedural awareness can draw on established coordination sequences to initiate rapid responses, even in unfamiliar situations.

PM supports creative responses by making familiar action patterns available as building blocks for recombination \citep{ciborra1996Improvisation}. When existing procedures prove insufficient for addressing unprecedented contingencies, individuals with well-developed procedural awareness can adapt, hybridize, and recombine known routines to construct novel approaches. In large-scale construction projects, cross-disciplinary procedural awareness expands the repertoire of action templates available for creative recombination, enabling members to draw on a wider range of procedural elements when composing improvised responses. Accordingly, we propose:

H5a: PM is positively associated with immediacy.

H5b: PM is positively associated with creativity.

\subsubsection{The mediating role of meta-knowledge}
DCTs provide the informational foundation for meta-knowledge development. Meta-knowledge, functioning as cognitive indexing, in turn enables rapid mobilization of distributed knowledge to support improvisation. Temporary team composition and limited shared work history suggest that team members cannot rely on gradually accumulated interpersonal familiarity to locate and mobilize distributed expertise \citep{jiang2025Confronting}. Meta-knowledge therefore serves as the cognitive bridge between the information generated through DCT use and the capacity for improvised action. Based on the differential patterns argued above, SM is expected to function as the most consistent mediator, because the visible and retrievable cognitive traces generated by both work-oriented and social-oriented use most directly support content-based expertise mapping, which in turn facilitates both rapid knowledge mobilization (immediacy) and cross-boundary knowledge recombination (creativity).

Based on the direct-effect arguments developed above, SM mediates the work-oriented use--improvisation capability relationship because the task-content traces generated by work-oriented use provide the informational foundation for content-based expertise mapping, which in turn supports both rapid knowledge mobilization and cross-boundary knowledge recombination. RM mediation operates because work-oriented use externalizes task interaction patterns that reveal coordination relationships, enabling pathway-based knowledge access. PM mediation operates because work-oriented use externalizes sequential process traces that make procedural patterns cognitively available as action templates \citep{zhang2018Mobile}. Accordingly, we propose:

H6a: SM mediates the relationship between work-oriented use and improvisation capability.

H6b: RM mediates the relationship between work-oriented use and improvisation capability.

H6c: PM mediates the relationship between work-oriented use and improvisation capability.

For social-oriented use, SM mediation operates because social-oriented use externalizes informal cognitive cues that complement content-based expertise mapping from a different angle than work-oriented use. RM mediation operates because social-oriented use directly externalizes relational cognition, making trust-based pathways and interpersonal connections observable as cognitive artifacts \citep{nisula2016Role}. PM mediation operates because social-oriented use externalizes informal procedural insights from social exchanges, providing tacit norms and practical adaptations that complement formal process awareness. Accordingly, we propose:

H6d: SM mediates the relationship between social-oriented use and improvisation capability.

H6e: RM mediates the relationship between social-oriented use and improvisation capability.

H6f: PM mediates the relationship between social-oriented use and improvisation capability.

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Data collection}
To enhance the reliability and validity of the survey instrument, a pilot study was conducted prior to the formal data collection. Using a snowball sampling approach, employees with experience using DCTs in project settings were recruited through the research team's professional networks. A total of 47 valid responses were obtained. The pilot results indicated satisfactory psychometric properties for all measurement scales (Cronbach’s $\alpha$ > 0.70), supporting their suitability for the main study. Prior to formal data collection, an a priori power analysis using G*Power 3.1 \citep{faul2009Statistical} was conducted. Based on a medium effect size ($f^{2}$ = 0.15), a significance level of $\alpha$ = .05, and statistical power of .95, with six predictors in the most complex structural equation, the minimum required sample size was determined to be 146. 

The formal data collection employed a three-wave time-lagged survey design to reduce common method bias \citep{podsakoff2003Common}. Data were collected from participants involved in large-scale infrastructure projects in China, including residential buildings, public buildings, infrastructures, and industrial buildings. The formal survey was distributed through a Chinese construction training organization that provides government-recognized certification programs for project professionals. The organization's trainee and alumni network include practitioners from diverse organizational roles and project types, providing access to a broad and heterogeneous sample. 

In the Chinese construction context, teams predominantly rely on three digital collaboration platforms---QQ, WeChat, and DingTalk---for daily coordination and communication \citep{zhu2023Dynamics, jia2025Knowledge}. The survey therefore assessed respondents' tool use specifically within these three digital environments. Only respondents who had been involved in their current projects for at least six months and who reported regular use of digital communication tools for project collaboration were invited to participate.

At Time 1, respondents were asked to report demographic information and DCT usage patterns (work-oriented use and social-oriented use). A total of 567 questionnaires were distributed, and 409 valid responses were returned, yielding an effective response rate of 72.1\%. One month later, the Time 2 survey was administered to respondents who had provided valid responses at Time 1. This wave measured meta-knowledge variables, including structural, relational, and procedural meta-knowledge. A total of 313 valid responses were collected at Time 2, representing a retention rate of 76.5\% relative to the Time 1 sample. Another month later, the Time 3 survey measured improvisation capability, including immediacy and creativity. To match responses across the three waves, respondents were asked to provide the last four digits of their mobile phone numbers as a unique identifier. After matching all three survey waves and excluding cases with missing or inconsistent data, the final sample comprised 255 valid responses, representing an overall retention rate of 62.3\% from Time 1. Descriptive statistics of the sample are presented in Table 2.

To ensure data quality, several measures were implemented. First, the professional survey company conducted IP address checks to prevent multiple submissions and removed responses with straight-lining patterns or insufficient response time (less than 50\% of median completion time). Second, an attention check question was embedded in the survey to verify that respondents were reading the questions carefully.

\subsection{Bias control and evaluation}
All participants were informed about the purpose of the study, the confidentiality of their responses, and their right to withdraw at any time. Informed consent was obtained from all participants prior to data collection. Several procedural remedies were adopted to mitigate common method bias \citep{podsakoff2003Common}. First, the three-wave design ensured that independent variables, mediators, and dependent variables were measured at separate time points, preventing respondents from inferring the expected relationships among constructs. Following prior research on project-based organizations, a one-month interval was adopted between consecutive survey waves. Research suggests that a time lag of one to six months is appropriate for capturing behavioral and cognitive changes in organizational settings \citep{carmeli2021Resilience, zaman2024Stress}. In our context, one month was deemed sufficient for DCT usage patterns to shape meta-knowledge through repeated daily interaction and coordination cycles, while remaining short enough to ensure that respondents were still engaged in the same ongoing projects---a condition reinforced by the eligibility requirement of at least six months of current project involvement. Second, respondents were assured that their responses were anonymous and would be used solely for academic research purposes. Third, survey instructions explicitly stated that there were no right or wrong answers and encouraged respondents to answer based on their actual experiences.

In addition to these procedural remedies, two statistical tests were conducted to evaluate the severity of common method bias. First, Harman's single-factor test was performed by subjecting all 24 measurement items to an unrotated principal component analysis. The results yielded seven factors with eigenvalues greater than 1.0, with the first factor accounting for 32.38\% of the total variance, well below the 40\% threshold \citep{podsakoff2003Common}. Second, a confirmatory factor analysis compared the hypothesized seven-factor measurement model (see Section 4.1) with a single-factor model in which all items loaded onto one latent factor. The single-factor model exhibited significantly poorer fit ($\chi^{2}$(252) = 1802.07, CFI = 0.496, TLI = 0.448, RMSEA = 0.155, SRMR = 0.124), and the chi-square difference test confirmed the superiority of the seven-factor model ($\Delta\chi^{2}$(21) = 1418.28, p < 0.001). Together, these results suggest that common method bias is unlikely to pose a serious threat to the validity of the findings.

To examine potential non-response bias, the final sample was divided into early and late respondent groups based on the order of questionnaire receipt \citep{armstrong1977Estimating}. Independent-samples t-tests and chi-square tests were conducted to compare the two groups on key demographic variables. The results indicated no significant differences between early and late respondents in gender ($\chi^{2}$(1) = 0.476, p = 0.490), age (t = $-$0.502, p = 0.616), educational level ($\chi^{2}$(3) = 1.662, p = 0.645), or work experience (t = 0.055, p = 0.957). These results suggest that non-response bias is not a significant concern in this study.

\subsection{Measures}
To ensure measurement rigor, all constructs in this study were operationalized using established scales adapted from existing research. To ensure contextual appropriateness and construct equivalence in the Chinese project setting, all measurement items were translated using a back-translation procedure. The translation process involved independent forward translation into Chinese, back-translation into English, and reconciliation by bilingual researchers with expertise in project management and organizational behavior. Discrepancies were discussed and resolved to ensure conceptual consistency with the original scales while maintaining relevance to large-scale project contexts in China. All items were measured using a five-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (“strongly disagree”) to 5 (“strongly agree”). 

DCT usage was measured using eight items adapted from \citet{chen2020Integrating}, including four items capturing work-oriented use and four capturing social-oriented use. Structural and relational meta-knowledge were each assessed using three-item scales adapted from \citet{engelbrecht2019How}. Procedural meta-knowledge was measured using three items adapted from \citet{faraj2000Coordinating} and modified to reflect the project context. Improvisation capability was measured using seven items from \citet{magni2009Role}, comprising three items for immediacy and four for creativity. All items were adapted to the construction project context and are reported in the Appendix.

Prior research has shown that individual characteristics such as gender, age, and educational background are important determinants of IC \citep{koutsoupidou2005Improvisation, magni2009Role}. In addition, project type and professional qualifications may influence both meta-knowledge formation and improvisation capability, as different project contexts present varying levels of complexity and standardization, and different professional roles entail distinct levels of decision-making authority and coordination responsibility. Accordingly, gender, age, educational background, project type, and professional qualifications are set as control variables.


\section{Results}
\subsection{Reliability and validity}
To assess the reliability and validity of the measurement instruments, confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) was conducted using R lavaan \citep{foss2011Linking, pesämaa2021Publishing}. The proposed seven-factor measurement model demonstrates an acceptable fit to the data ($\chi^{2}$(231) = 383.79, $\chi^{2}$/df = 1.661; CFI = 0.947; TLI = 0.936; RMSEA = 0.051, 90\% CI [0.042, 0.060]; SRMR = 0.049). All indices meet commonly accepted thresholds ($\chi^{2}$/df < 3, CFI and TLI > 0.90, RMSEA < 0.08, SRMR < 0.08), indicating adequate model fit.

The internal consistency of the constructs was examined using Cronbach's $\alpha$ and composite reliability (CR). As reported in Table 3, Cronbach's $\alpha$ values range from 0.797 to 0.838, and CR values range from 0.816 to 0.860, all exceeding the recommended threshold of 0.70. These results suggest satisfactory internal reliability across all constructs \citep{eisinga2019Building}.

Convergent validity was assessed using the average variance extracted (AVE). As shown in Table 3, the AVE values range from 0.527 to 0.672. All values exceed the recommended cutoff of 0.50, indicating that the measurement items adequately capture the variance of their corresponding latent constructs. Discriminant validity was evaluated following the Fornell-Larcker criterion. As presented in Table 3, the square root of the AVE for each construct is greater than its correlations with all other constructs, providing evidence of satisfactory discriminant validity \citep{chen2007Sensitivity, li2019When}.

\subsection{Hypothesis testing}
To test the proposed hypotheses, a covariance-based structural equation modeling (CB-SEM) approach was employed to obtain robust parameter estimations for the complex research model. The results of the structural model are presented in Tables 4 and 5.

The empirical results indicate that WU has a significant positive effect on SM ($\beta$ = 0.247, 95\% CI = [0.141,0.353], p < 0.001) and PM ($\beta$ = 0.294, p < 0.001), thereby supporting H1a and H1c. However, the effect of WU on RM is insignificant ($\beta$ = 0.060, p > 0.05), and thus H1b is not supported. Similarly, SU shows significant positive effects on SM ($\beta$ = 0.204, p < 0.001) and RM ($\beta$ = 0.193, p < 0.001), supporting H2a and H2b. In contrast, the effect of SU on PM is insignificant ($\beta$ = 0.036, p > 0.05), not supporting H2c.

Regarding the effects of meta-knowledge on IC, SM exhibits significant positive effects on both IMM ($\beta$ = 0.205, 95\% CI = [0.092,0.318], p < 0.001), and CRE ($\beta$ = 0.136, 95\% CI = [0.028, 0.244], p < 0.05), supporting H3a and H3b. RM demonstrates a significant positive association with IMM ($\beta$ = 0.174, 95\% CI = [0.066, 0.282], p < 0.01). However, the 95\% confidence interval for the effect of RM on CRE includes zero ($\beta$ = 0.030, 95\% CI = [-0.074, 0.133], p > 0.1), indicating that H4a is supported whereas H4b is not. PM shows a significant positive effect on CRE ($\beta$ = 0.256, 95\% CI = [0.156,0.357], p < 0.001) but does not significantly affect IMM ($\beta$ = 0.009, 95\% CI = [-0.096, 0.113], p > 0.1), Therefore, H5b is supported, while H5a is rejected.

Among the control variables, only age showed a significant negative effect on IMM ($\beta$ = $-$0.101, p = 0.031). Gender, educational background, project type, and professional qualifications had no significant effects on either IMM or CRE (all p > 0.05).

Bootstrapping with 5,000 resamples was conducted to assess the significance of the indirect effects, and the results are reported in Table 5. For the WU pathways, the indirect effects through SM on both IMM ($\beta$ = 0.051, 95\% CI = [0.018, 0.089]) and CRE ($\beta$ = 0.034, 95\% CI = [0.004, 0.070]) are significant. H6a is therefore supported: SM mediates the relationship between WU and IC. In contrast, the 95\% confidence intervals for the indirect effects through RM include zero for both IMM ($\beta$ = 0.010, 95\% CI = [$-$0.013, 0.035]) and CRE ($\beta$ = 0.002, 95\% CI = [$-$0.007, 0.015]). H6b is thus not supported. For PM, the indirect effect of WU on CRE is significant ($\beta$ = 0.075, 95\% CI = [0.034, 0.128]), whereas the indirect effect on IMM is not ($\beta$ = 0.003, 95\% CI = [$-$0.038, 0.040]). H6c is therefore partially supported: PM mediates the WU--CRE relationship but not the WU--IMM relationship.

For the SU pathways, the indirect effects through SM are significant for both IMM ($\beta$ = 0.042, 95\% CI = [0.013, 0.079]) and CRE ($\beta$ = 0.028, 95\% CI = [0.002, 0.059]). H6d is therefore supported: SM mediates the relationship between SU and IC. The indirect effect through RM is significant for IMM ($\beta$ = 0.034, 95\% CI = [0.008, 0.069]) but not for CRE ($\beta$ = 0.006, 95\% CI = [$-$0.017, 0.030]). H6e is thus partially supported: RM mediates the SU--IMM relationship but not the SU--CRE relationship. For PM, neither the indirect effect on IMM ($\beta$ = 0.001, 95\% CI = [$-$0.009, 0.010]) nor on CRE ($\beta$ = 0.009, 95\% CI = [$-$0.023, 0.043]) is significant. H6f is therefore not supported.

\subsection{Endogeneity and robustness checks}
To enhance the robustness of our findings, we conducted supplementary analyses. After controlling for demographic and contextual variables, a simplified model excluding meta-knowledge variables showed that both WU and SU remained significantly positively associated with IC (IMM and CRE), indicating robust main effects.

To examine potential endogeneity, we explored reverse causality. Reverse regression showed that improvisation capability positively predicts WU ($\beta$ = 0.195, p < 0.001), suggesting a mutually reinforcing relationship between digital tool use and team capability. This aligns with the co-evolution view of technology use and organizational capabilities. The Durbin–Wu–Hausman test for WU was significant (p < 0.05), indicating that a strictly unidirectional causal interpretation should be treated with caution \citep{becker2016Statistical}.

In summary, these analyses suggest that DCT usage and IC likely exhibit a mutually reinforcing relationship. While our theoretical model and time-lagged design support the proposed pathway, the endogeneity tests imply that teams with stronger improvisation capability may also more effectively leverage DCT. The meta-knowledge mediation mechanism revealed in this study should therefore be considered as a core mechanism within this broader dynamic relationship rather than as an exclusive causal chain. Future research using panel data or instrumental variables would help clarify causal direction and strengthen the robustness of the proposed model.

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{Main findings}
As summarized in Tables 4 and 5, the empirical analysis reveals that while most hypotheses are supported, several require further investigation. The following discussion examines these key findings in detail.

\subsubsection{The asymmetric effects of DCT usage on meta-knowledge}
The results reveal distinct patterns in how different DCTs usage orientations shape meta-knowledge. WU exhibits significant positive effects on SM and PM, but not on RM. In contrast, SU significantly enhances SM and RM, while showing no significant effect on PM. These asymmetric effects suggest that different DCTs usage fosters complementary yet distinct forms of meta-knowledge infrastructure. 

The differential effects of WU can be understood through its task-oriented nature. Work-focused interactions are typically structured around coordination, role clarity, and workflow execution---activities that naturally generate shared understanding of "who knows what" (SM) and "how work gets done" (PM) \citep{cho2022Digital, neff2010CASE}. Frequent task discussions, problem-solving, and process alignment continuously reinforce these structural and procedural knowledge components. However, the transactional and role-bound character of WU provides limited exposure to socio-emotional cues, team members' collaboration styles, or informal relationship dynamics. Without deeper interpersonal exchanges, relational awareness remains underdeveloped, explaining the non-significant effect on RM.

SU demonstrates a theoretically complementary pattern. Social interactions extend beyond formal task boundaries and provide socio-emotional cues that enhance understanding of team members' preferences, expectations, and informal networks \citep{brockhaus2023Digitalization, cho2022Digital}. This interpersonal familiarity directly strengthens RM. At the same time, the spontaneous and less task-focused nature of SU does not consistently produce stable or codifiable procedural knowledge, accounting for the non-significant relationship with PM. As a result, through their distinct yet complementary interaction patterns, both WU and SU tend to foster the development of SM, while exhibiting complementary effects on RM and PM.

\subsubsection{The differentiated effects of meta-knowledge on IC}
The results consistently show that SM is positively associated with both IMM and CRE (H3a–H3b supported), indicating that shared awareness of expertise distribution benefits both dimensions of improvisation capability. More nuanced patterns emerge for RM and PM, whose effects display a clear asymmetry across the two outcomes. RM is positively associated with IMM but shows no significant relationship with CRE (H4a supported; H4b not supported). In contrast, PM exerts a significant positive effect on CRE but not on IMM (H5b supported; H5a not supported). Taken together, the three meta-knowledge dimensions do not contribute to IC in a uniform manner, but exhibit distinct and complementary relationships with IMM and CRE.

The above results suggest that, although improvisation requires teams to integrate distributed knowledge resources under time pressure and uncertainty, the mechanisms differ across IMM and CRE. A knowledge integration perspective provides a coherent explanation for this asymmetric pattern \citep{faraj2000Coordinating, perry-smith2017Creativity}. In this context, SM appears to function as a general integration infrastructure that supports both outcomes, while RM and PM correspond to more specialized integration pathways. This asymmetry reflects the efficiency–innovation tension inherent in DCT-enabled collaboration. IMM relies primarily on rapid mobilization and alignment of relevant expertise, whereas CRE depends more strongly on the recombination of heterogeneous knowledge inputs. Prior research helps explain why these integration mechanisms operate differently. The findings of \citet{soda2019Network} indicate that coordination-oriented interaction structures tend to reinforce shared understanding and streamline communication, but may simultaneously reduce exposure to diverse knowledge domains that enable novel recombination. Conversely, process-oriented knowledge provides a repertoire of routines that can be flexibly recombined, which supports creative outcomes. However, such procedural integration does not necessarily translate into faster action under urgency. In time-critical situations, procedural templates alone are insufficient if teams cannot rapidly align roles, coordinate interdependent actions, and enact accountability in real time. \citet{okhuysen200910} showed that IMM depends more strongly on immediate alignment and accountability than on formalized procedures alone, providing further support for this interpretation. The knowledge integration perspective, combined with these established arguments in prior research, offers a plausible explanation for the differentiated support observed across H3a–H5b.

\subsubsection{The mediating role of meta-knowledge in DCT-IC}
Although DCT usage shows significant relationships with multiple types of meta-knowledge, only a limited set of mediation paths is supported. Specifically, SM consistently transmits the effects of both WU and SU to IC (H6a, H6d), whereas RM and PM operate as mediators only in selective paths (H6e partially supported; H6c partially supported). This uneven pattern suggests that the cognitive consequences of DCT interaction may be more constrained and context-dependent than originally anticipated.

A team cognition perspective provides a coherent explanation for this pattern. Prior research has shown that shared cognitive structures do not emerge uniformly but develop unevenly depending on the informational cues embedded in interaction \citep{ren2011Transactive, zhichengliu2008Distributed}. DCTs are not neutral conduits of knowledge. Instead, they shape which cognitive structures are most likely to form through repeated interaction. The findings of \citet{majchrzak2013Contradictory} support this view by demonstrating that digital interaction generates persistent and highly visible traces of communication and problem solving. Such visibility makes expertise signaling and knowledge location particularly salient in digital environments. This perspective helps explain why SM emerges as the dominant and universal mediator. Persistent communication traces make it easier for team members to recognize and remember the distribution of expertise over time \citep{chen2020Integrating}. In contrast, RM and PM rely on different types of interactional cues that are not consistently produced through DCT usage. Relational meta-knowledge depends on socio-emotional and contextual information that typically extends beyond task-focused interaction. Procedural meta-knowledge develops through repeated engagement in workflow execution and process coordination \citep{marion2021Transformation}. As a result, the cognitive consequences of digital interaction remain uneven, leading to selective mediation effects.

These findings suggest that the relationship between digital collaboration and performance is more contingent than often assumed. Digital interaction generates abundant communication, but only some of this interaction crystallizes into shared cognitive structures that can support improvisation capability \citep{ye2021Leveraging, zaverzhenets2021Digitalising}. 

\subsection{Theoretical implications}
\subsubsection{Reframing digital collaboration as cognitive infrastructure building}
This study reframes digital collaboration in project research by showing that digital communication tools primarily build team cognitive infrastructure rather than merely improve communication efficiency. Prior project management research has largely treated digital tool use as a homogeneous driver of coordination and information sharing outcomes \citep{faraj2000Coordinating, leonardi2015Ambient, turner2022Improving}. The present findings show that digital collaboration does not produce uniform effects; instead, its consequences depend fundamentally on how digital tools are used within project teams.

Distinguishing between work-oriented and social-oriented tool use reveals that different interaction orientations cultivate distinct forms of shared understanding. Task-focused interaction primarily strengthens awareness of expertise distribution and work processes, whereas socially oriented interaction reinforces relational awareness across team members. Digital collaboration therefore operates as a system through which project teams construct shared cognitive foundations that enable coordinated action. By shifting attention from technology adoption to interaction orientation, this study positions digital collaboration as a cognitive infrastructure-building process that underpins team capability in complex project environments \citep{leonardi2015Ambient, majchrzak2013Contradictory}.

\subsubsection{Contribution to meta-knowledge and distributed cognition perspective}
The findings extend distributed cognition and project knowledge research by showing that team capability depends not only on knowledge possession but also on the ability to mobilize distributed knowledge. Traditional project research has emphasized knowledge acquisition, expertise, and learning as primary drivers of performance \citep{hutchins2020Distributed, leonardi2014Social, oostervink2016Knowledge}. This perspective implicitly assumes that teams perform better when members possess more knowledge or higher levels of expertise.

The present study shows that, in digitally mediated project environments, the ability to locate, combine, and mobilize distributed knowledge becomes as important as knowledge possession and can be even more critical under time pressure \citep{mohammadparsttabas2024Igniting, moser2021Knowledge, jun2022Innovation}. Team effectiveness therefore relies on shared awareness of how knowledge is distributed, how it can be accessed, and how it is enacted in practice. This perspective extends distributed cognition perspective by highlighting knowledge mobilization as a central foundation of team capability.

The study further strengthens this theoretical shift by conceptualizing procedural meta-knowledge as a distinct dimension of team cognition. Existing research has primarily focused on awareness of expertise and relational connections, while shared understanding of how work is carried out in practice has remained under-theorized \citep{orlikowski200810, luo1202How, ren2011Transactive, wegner1987Theories}. The findings demonstrate that shared procedural understanding enables teams to recombine routines and respond creatively to unexpected situations. By identifying this cognitive mechanism, the study expands the conceptualization of team cognition and clarifies how distributed knowledge becomes actionable during project execution.

\subsubsection{Inconsistent DCT usage findings through differentiated pathways}
This study contributes to the digital collaboration literature by explaining why prior research has reported inconsistent and context-dependent outcomes. Previous studies have alternately emphasized the coordination benefits of digital tools and the new constraints they introduce, resulting in fragmented conclusions regarding their overall effectiveness \citep{majchrzak2013Contradictory, kane2014Whats, sun2021Dark}.

The present findings demonstrate that digital collaboration influences team capability through differentiated cognitive pathways rather than through a single uniform mechanism. Work-oriented and social-oriented interactions cultivate complementary but distinct forms of shared understanding, which in turn exert selective effects on improvisation capability. This pathway perspective clarifies how digital interaction can simultaneously enable coordination and generate uneven outcomes across teams and contexts.

By identifying structural, relational, and procedural meta-knowledge as the cognitive mechanisms through which digital interaction shapes team capability, the study provides a coherent explanation for the mixed findings in prior research. This perspective shifts the theoretical focus from whether digital collaboration improves performance to how digital collaboration reshapes team cognitive infrastructures that enable capability development.

\subsection{Practical implications}
This study provides practical insights for project owners and frontline managers seeking to use DCTs to support improvisation capability in large-scale construction projects.

Firstly, managing DCT usage as a knowledge-building process, not merely a communication channel. Our findings show that work-oriented use primarily builds structural and procedural meta-knowledge, whereas social-oriented use primarily builds structural and relational meta-knowledge. Because single-mode interaction creates meta-knowledge blind spots, project managers should design digital collaboration environments that combine both task-focused coordination and informal interaction spaces.

Secondly, work-focused DCT practices should systematically externalize expertise and work processes. In temporary project teams lacking shared history, participants rely on digital traces to understand “who knows what” and “how work gets done.” WU develops SM and PM by making task coordination, decision processes, and expertise distribution visible. Practices such as maintaining searchable project logs, documenting decision processes, and creating expertise directories enable teams to rapidly mobilize knowledge when unexpected situations arise.

Thirdly, creating dedicated spaces for informal interaction to build relational awareness. SU strengthens relational meta-knowledge, which supports rapid knowledge mobilization under time pressure---particularly critical in large scale   projects with fluid membership and pronounced organizational boundaries. Digital environments should therefore accommodate both structured work communication and relationship-building interactions, enabling teams to efficiently navigate interpersonal networks during urgent situations.

Furthermore, aligning meta-knowledge-building strategies with specific capability demands. According to the findings that RM supports improvisation immediacy, whereas PM supports improvisation creativity. In phases demanding rapid response, managers should prioritize practices that enhance expertise visibility and relationship networking. In phases requiring creative problem-solving, managers should focus on capturing and sharing procedural knowledge through lessons-learned repositories and structured reflection sessions.

Finally, recognize that digital interaction alone does not automatically generate meta-knowledge. Structural meta-knowledge emerges as the most consistent mediator linking DCT usage to improvisation, whereas RM and PM require more intentional design. Project leaders should therefore implement mechanisms that systematically externalize expertise distribution---such as skill-tagging systems and expertise directories---while creating spaces for informal exchange and relationship-building. By treating digital collaboration as designed infrastructure rather than an emergent byproduct, organizations can build the complementary knowledge foundations required for effective improvisation.

\subsection{Limitations and future research directions}
This study has several limitations should be acknowledged. A first limitation concerns the project context in which the proposed meta-knowledge pathways were examined. Large-scale construction projects are characterized by temporary,  and highly interdependent organizational structures. These features make meta-knowledge particularly critical for coordination and improvisation. However, the prominence of meta-knowledge observed in this study may be partly context-specific. In projects with more stable membership, higher task modularity, or stronger technological integration---such as software development or R\&D teams---DCT usage may generate different cognitive consequences. Future research could therefore examine how project characteristics shape the relative importance of different meta-knowledge pathways and explore the boundary conditions of the proposed framework across diverse project settings.

Another limitation relates to the temporal nature of meta-knowledge development. Although the time-lagged design provides separation between predictors and outcomes, the study captures meta-knowledge as a relatively stable cognitive structure. In practice, meta-knowledge is likely to emerge, decay, and be reconfigured through ongoing digital interaction, team turnover, and shifting project demands. Understanding how these cognitive structures evolve over time represents an important next step. Longitudinal and process-based research could examine how meta-knowledge pathways unfold across project phases and how digital collaboration reshapes team cognition dynamically.

The study also relies on perceptual measures to capture meta-knowledge and digital collaboration. While appropriate for examining shared cognitive structures, this approach cannot fully capture the behavioral traces through which meta-knowledge develops in digital environments. Future research could combine survey and digital trace data to better understand how interaction patterns translate into shared cognitive infrastructures.

\section{Conclusion}
This study examines how digital communication tool use shapes large-scale construction project teams’ improvisation capability through meta-knowledge development. Drawing on distributed cognition perspective, the study develops and tests a theoretical model that distinguishes work-oriented and social-oriented DCT usage and conceptualizes structural, relational, and procedural meta-knowledge as key cognitive mechanisms linking digital interaction to team capability. The model is examined using time-lagged survey data collected from members of large-scale construction project teams.
The findings reveal that DCT usage does not influence improvisation capability uniformly but operates through differentiated meta-knowledge pathways. Work-oriented and social-oriented DCT usage foster distinct yet complementary forms of meta-knowledge, while structural meta-knowledge emerges as the most consistent mechanism linking digital collaboration to both dimensions of improvisation capability. Relational and procedural meta-knowledge exhibit more selective effects, highlighting the uneven cognitive consequences of digital interaction. Together, these results demonstrate that the impact of DCT usage depends on how digital interaction reshapes team cognitive infrastructures rather than on technology use alone.

These insights contribute to research on digital collaboration, distributed cognition, and project teamwork by clarifying the cognitive mechanisms through which digital interaction translates into team capability. The findings also offer guidance for project organizations seeking to design digital collaboration practices that support both rapid response and creative problem solving in complex project environments. Because the empirical context focuses on construction and infrastructure project teams in China, caution is warranted when generalizing the findings to other project settings and national contexts.
