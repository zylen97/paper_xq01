\begin{abstract}
Improvisation capability enables project teams to respond effectively to unexpected situations, yet how digital communication tool use shapes this capability remains underexplored. This study develops and tests a model linking work-oriented and social-oriented digital communication tool use to improvisation capability through meta-knowledge---the project team's collective awareness of expertise, relationships, and procedures. Three-wave time-lagged survey data from 255 large-scale construction project teams in China show that different usage patterns foster complementary forms of meta-knowledge, which selectively transmit effects to improvisation capability. Structural meta-knowledge emerges as the dominant mediator, whereas relational and procedural meta-knowledge exhibit more selective effects. This research contributes by distinguishing work-oriented from social-oriented tool use, introducing procedural meta-knowledge as a novel construct, and identifying differentiated pathways linking digital collaboration to improvisation capability. The findings offer practical guidance for designing digital collaboration practices that enhance team capability in uncertain project environments.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Large-scale construction projects, characterized by long cycles, complex technical interdependencies, and multiple-stakeholder involvement \citep{yang2025How,mariano2024Managing}, often encounter uncertainties \citep{shen2022Largescale}. Traditional plan-driven project management approaches are challenged by the need for quick responses to contingencies \citep{malucelli2021Facing,coulon2026Wing}. Project teams' improvisation capability has emerged as a critical ability for addressing these challenges \citep{jiang2025Confronting,sumbal2025Improvisation}. \textbf{\textit{Improvisation capability}} refers to the ability to spontaneously create and implement solutions to unexpected project contingencies by rapidly mobilizing and recombining existing knowledge and resources \citep{magni2009Role,veranghg2005Improvisation}. For instance, the final joint of the Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macao Bridge immersed tunnel exceeded its acceptance tolerance. In response, the project members improvised an unprecedented reverse-operation to re-dock the structure, reducing the deviation from 17 centimeters to 2.5 millimeters within 38 hours \citep{21cbh2018Redocking}. The convergence of planning and execution in real time and the generation of novel responses distinguish improvisation capability from routine problem-solving \citep{moorman1998Organizational, miner2001Organizational}. Enhancing project teams' improvisation capability is of practical importance, especially given the difficulty of comprehensive planning in a temporary organization setting \citep{wang2025Formalization,jiang2023Toward}.

Existing project management research on antecedents of improvisation capability in project teams has concentrated on three areas, as shown in Table 1. First, cognitive and psychological states, such as collective mindfulness \citep{jiang2025Confronting}, psychological safety \citep{huang2026Thriving}, and facilitating conditions \citep{coulon2026Wing}, have been shown to enable improvisation capability. Second, the knowledge and experience base, including tacit knowledge and sensemaking \citep{sumbal2025Improvisation} and ambidextrous search \citep{rui2022Contingent, klein2015Improvisation}, contributes to improvisation capability development. Third, contextual and environmental antecedents, such as project complexity \citep{mamedio2020Managing, jiang2023Toward}, plan deviations \citep{vuorinen2019Lifecycle}, and uncertainty \citep{hacker2019Legitimizing}, prompt improvisational responses. Yet, limited study has examined the role of communication technology through which knowledge is shared and mobilized.

Effective improvisation depends on the real-time mobilization of distributed knowledge \citep{magni2009Role, moorman1998Organizational}. Compared with general projects, large-scale construction projects involve a far greater number of participating organizations \citep{hu2024Performance}, deeper cross-disciplinary knowledge heterogeneity \citep{yang2025How}, and wider geographical dispersion of work sites \citep{shen2022Largescale}. These characteristics amplify the challenge of real-time knowledge mobilization. When unexpected events arise, conventional communication mechanisms, such as scheduled meetings and formal documentation, cannot operate at the speed that improvisation demands. \textbf{\textit{Digital communication tools (DCTs)}} are well positioned to address this problem. As the primary medium to coordinate and exchange expertise in real time, DCTs enable team members to instantly reach across organizational and geographical boundaries to locate relevant knowledge and mobilize it for immediate action \citep{zhang2018Mobile}. In practice, mobile social media platforms such as WhatsApp, Microsoft Teams, WeChat, and Slack have become essential for cross-organizational coordination, mediating task assignment, technical problem-solving, and informal exchange \citep{ma2022Investigating, jia2025Knowledge}. However, the project management literature has not examined whether and how DCT use contributes to the project team's improvisation capability. 

Moreover, DCT use in construction project teams is not a homogeneous behavior. In practice, team members use DCTs both for task-focused coordination, such as assigning work, solving technical problems, and tracking progress, and for relationship-oriented exchange, such as sharing experiences, seeking informal advice, and maintaining interpersonal connections. Prior research in the construction project context has confirmed this distinction, showing that work-oriented and social-oriented use patterns produce different effects on project outcomes \citep{ma2022Understanding, jiang2026Bolstering}. Yet how these distinct usage patterns differentially contribute to improvisation capability remains unexplored. \textbf{\textit{Therefore, the first aim of this study is to examine how work-oriented and social-oriented DCT use differentially influence improvisation capability in large-scale construction projects.}}

A critical problem, then, is through what cognitive mechanism these distinct DCT use patterns translate into improvisation capability. In large-scale construction projects, DCTs generate a substantial volume of information flows across organizational boundaries \citep{treem2013Social}. These flows increase the knowledge density accessible to each team member \citep{leonardi2015Ambient}. Improvisation demands that relevant expertise be located and mobilized within extremely compressed time frames. Without team-level awareness of who holds what knowledge and how it can be accessed, team members risk being overwhelmed by abundant but undirected information \citep{sun2021Dark}. They cannot convert the knowledge available through DCTs into timely improvised action. This cognitive resource---the project team's collective awareness of how knowledge is distributed and accessible within the team---is what the literature terms \textbf{\textit{meta-knowledge}} \citep{wegner1987Transactive, leonardi2014Social}. Yet, the project management literature has predominantly examined knowledge itself, focusing on knowledge sharing, knowledge transfer, and knowledge integration as enablers of project performance \citep{jia2025Knowledge, hu2024Performance}, while paying limited attention to meta-knowledge. Without understanding how project teams develop their members' awareness of knowledge distribution and access, the field cannot explain the cognitive foundation of improvised action in large-scale construction projects. Research in information systems has demonstrated that enterprise social media can enhance meta-knowledge through communication visibility \citep{leonardi2014Social, engelbrecht2019How}. Yet the project management field has not examined how DCT use cultivates this cognitive resource, nor how different usage patterns may do so through distinct pathways. \textbf{\textit{Therefore, the second aim of this study is to examine how meta-knowledge serves as the bridging cognitive mechanism through which work-oriented and social-oriented DCT use influence improvisation capability.}}

This study examines how work-oriented and social-oriented DCT use influence improvisation capability through the development of meta-knowledge in large-scale construction projects. We integrate the characteristics of large-scale construction project teams (highly dispersed expertise, limited shared work history, and time-compressed coordination) with the meta-knowledge literature \citep{wegner1987Transactive, leonardi2014Social}. On this basis, we examine three dimensions of team members' meta-knowledge: structural meta-knowledge (knowing who knows what), relational meta-knowledge (knowing who knows whom), and procedural meta-knowledge (knowing how to access and mobilize knowledge). We test the proposed model using three-wave time-lagged survey data from 255 large-scale construction project teams in China, with one key informant per team. This study draws on distributed cognition perspective \citep{hutchins2020Distributed}, which conceptualizes cognition as emerging from interactions among individuals, technological artifacts, and the social environment. This theoretical lens is particularly suited to our context because it treats DCTs not merely as passive channels but as active constituents of the cognitive system through which meta-knowledge is developed and improvisation capability is enacted \citep{leonardi2015Ambient}. Accordingly, this study addresses the following research questions (RQs):

\textbf{RQ1:} \textit{How do work-oriented and social-oriented digital communication tool use influence improvisation capability in large-scale construction projects?}

\textbf{RQ2:} \textit{Does meta-knowledge mediate the relationship between digital communication tool use and improvisation capability?}

The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. Section 2 presents the theoretical foundations and develops the research hypotheses. Section 3 describes the methodology. Section 4 presents the results. Section 5 discusses the findings and their implications. Section 6 concludes with limitations and directions for future research.

\section{Theoretical foundations and hypotheses development}
\subsection{Improvisation capability in large-scale construction projects}
The concept of improvisation originated from analogies between jazz performance and organizational action, with early work defining improvisation primarily as the convergence of planning and execution \citep{moorman1998Organizational}. Subsequent scholarship further reconceptualized improvisation as a process in which existing resources and knowledge are creatively recombined to produce novel responses under constraints \citep{miner2001Organizational, crossan2003Making}. Integrating both the temporal and knowledge-recombination perspectives, \citet{veranghg2005Improvisation} and \citet{magni2009Role} operationalized improvisation capability as comprising two dimensions: \textbf{\textit{immediacy and creativity}}. \textbf{\textit{Immediacy}} captures the capacity to formulate and implement solutions with minimal delay between conception and action, reflecting the project team's responsiveness under time pressure \citep{veranghg2005Improvisation}. \textbf{\textit{Creativity}} reflects the ability to generate novel solutions through non-routine recombination of available knowledge and resources, producing outcomes that go beyond established procedures \citep{magni2009Role}. This two-dimensional conceptualization suggests that effective improvisation requires not merely speed of response, but also the quality and originality of the solution produced under temporal constraints. 

In the project management domain, improvisation capability has received growing scholarly attention, particularly in large-scale construction projects where technical complexity, cross-organizational workforce composition, and geographical dispersion of work sites amplify both the frequency of unexpected contingencies and the difficulty of improvised response \citep{klein2015Improvisation, malucelli2021Facing, jiang2023Toward}. Table~\ref{tab:antecedents} synthesizes the existing research on antecedents of improvisation capability in the project management literature, suggesting three categories. First, cognitive and psychological states, demonstrate that conditions such as collective mindfulness \citep{jiang2025Confronting, wang2025Formalization}, psychological safety \citep{huang2026Thriving}, and facilitating conditions \citep{coulon2026Wing} create a state of readiness that enables spontaneous action. Second, knowledge and experience base, establishes that improvisation draws on existing knowledge stocks, including tacit knowledge \citep{sumbal2025Improvisation}, bricolage \citep{xie2025How}, ambidextrous search \citep{rui2022Contingent}, and theoretical knowledge foundations \citep{klein2015Improvisation, nisula2016Role}. Third, contextual and environmental triggers, identify conditions such as project complexity \citep{mamedio2020Managing, jiang2023Toward}, plan deviations \citep{hamzeh2018Investigating, vuorinen2019Lifecycle}, and extreme uncertainty \citep{hacker2019Legitimizing, malucelli2021Facing} that necessitate improvisation. 

Despite these contributions, all three streams focus on the preconditions of improvisation (readiness, knowledge stocks, and triggering contexts), without examining the infrastructure through which distributed knowledge is located and mobilized during improvised action. Psychological readiness does not automatically translate into coordinated action across organizational boundaries. Possessing knowledge is not equivalent to mobilizing it under temporal constraints. None of these streams examines the communication tools through which project team members identify who holds relevant expertise, gain access to it, and recombine it for immediate action. The absence of communication technology from existing antecedent research is notable given the centrality of DCTs in contemporary project practice. In large-scale construction projects, DCTs have become the primary medium through which team members coordinate tasks, share technical knowledge, and reach across organizational boundaries in real time \citep{zhang2018Mobile, jia2025Knowledge}, constituting the primary infrastructure for cross-boundary knowledge mobilization \citep{ma2022Investigating}. A systematic examination of how DCT use shapes improvisation capability is therefore warranted.

\subsection{Digital communication tool use in project teams}
DCT research in organizational settings has established that visibility, persistence, editability, and association of DCTs can transform communication from ephemeral and dyadic exchanges into persistent and collectively visible traces \citep{treem2013Social}. These characteristics create ambient awareness of distributed activities and expertise \citep{leonardi2014Social}. In project contexts, a growing body of empirical work demonstrates the centrality of DCTs. \citet{zhang2018Mobile} showed that mobile social media platforms such as WeChat and WhatsApp serve as primary tools for cross-organizational virtual collaboration in inter-organizational projects. \citet{jia2025Knowledge} found that mobile social media groups have become the dominant channel for knowledge sharing within construction project teams, while \citet{ma2022Investigating} demonstrated that DCT-mediated communication facilitates cross-boundary coordination and collaborative problem-solving in construction projects. These studies establish that DCTs are deeply embedded in the daily work of construction project teams, yet research in this area has focused predominantly on coordination and performance outcomes of DCT use without differentiating how DCT use shapes team-level cognitive resources.

Research on technology use has increasingly distinguished between work-oriented and social-oriented patterns of engagement based on the purposes that guide user behavior \citep{ali-hassan2015Linking, cao2019Exploring}. Work-oriented use refers to purposeful, task-driven interactions such as searching for technical information, assigning tasks, discussing project problems, and coordinating schedules \citep{ma2022Investigating}. Social-oriented use refers to relationship-driven interactions such as sharing personal experiences, seeking informal advice, exchanging emotional support, and maintaining interpersonal connections \citep{ali-hassan2015Linking, huang2017Ties}. In construction project settings, these two patterns produce distinct effects: work-oriented use primarily enhances task performance, while social-oriented use strengthens relational coordination \citep{ma2022Understanding, jiang2026Bolstering}. These differential effects are likely amplified in large-scale construction projects, where more pronounced cross-organizational boundaries and wider geographical dispersion heighten the need for both task coordination and relationship maintenance. More recently, \citet{zhu2024Effects} demonstrated that enterprise social media use can enhance employees' improvisation ability through psychological mechanisms, providing initial evidence that DCT use is relevant to improvisation. However, how work-oriented and social-oriented use patterns differentially influence the cognitive resources that underpin improvisation capability in project teams, specifically meta-knowledge, remains unexplored.

\subsection{Meta-knowledge in project teams}
Meta-knowledge refers to a project team's collective awareness of how knowledge and expertise are distributed, connected, and mobilized within the team \citep{wegner1987Transactive}. In the transactive memory systems literature, meta-knowledge originated as the cognitive component that enables team members to locate relevant expertise without possessing it themselves \citep{lewis2003Measuring, kush2019Conceptual}. 

Three research streams contribute to the present understanding of meta-knowledge. First, transactive memory systems research established that effective team coordination depends on members' awareness of how expertise is distributed across the team \citep{wegner1987Transactive, lewis2003Measuring}. This dimension refers to "knowing which team members hold which domain-specific knowledge" \citep{kush2019Conceptual, ellwart2014Team}. We term this content-based awareness \textbf{\textit{structural meta-knowledge (SM)}}: the project team's collective awareness of how expertise and knowledge domains are distributed across team members, enabling efficient identification of appropriate knowledge sources. 

Second, social network and communication visibility research has demonstrated that individuals also develop awareness of the relational pathways through which knowledge can be accessed \citep{borgatti2003Relational, leonardi2014Social}. Studies of enterprise social media have shown that communication visibility enables individuals to perceive not only who knows what but also who knows whom, identifying the interpersonal ties and intermediaries through which expertise can be reached \citep{fu2025Enterprise, engelbrecht2019How}. We term this pathway-based awareness \textbf{\textit{relational meta-knowledge (RM)}}: the project team's collective awareness of social ties and interaction pathways through which distributed knowledge can be accessed. 

Third, team coordination research has highlighted that team members also develop awareness of how distributed expertise is typically mobilized and combined in practice \citep{faraj2000Coordinating}. The coordination dimension of TMS \citep{lewis2003Measuring, kush2019Conceptual}, interaction molding routines in complex project work \citep{korotkova2024Do}, and meta-knowledge of organizational processes \citep{barmeyer2019Informal} all point to a process-oriented facet of meta-knowledge. We term this action-oriented awareness \textbf{\textit{procedural meta-knowledge (PM)}}: the project team's collective awareness of how distributed expertise is typically mobilized, combined, and enacted, including cross-disciplinary problem-solving routines and established approaches for responding to recurring challenges.

Synthesizing these three streams, this study adopts a three-dimensional conceptualization of meta-knowledge encompassing SM, RM, and PM. Empirical meta-knowledge work has been conducted almost exclusively within stable organizational teams, such as R\&D groups, management teams, and entrepreneurial ventures \citep{mohammadparsttabas2024Igniting, ren2011Transactive, kush2019Conceptual}, where membership continuity allows knowledge structures to gradually stabilize. Research situated in project management contexts, particularly large-scale construction projects, remains scarce. Large-scale project teams face conditions that make meta-knowledge particularly important yet difficult to develop \citep{korotkova2024Do}. The present study thus proposes that meta-knowledge serves as the mediating mechanism through which DCT use influences improvisation capability.

\subsection{Distributed cognition perspective}
The distributed cognition perspective provides the theoretical foundation for this study. Originating from Hutchins' studies of navigation teams, this perspective advances three core propositions \citep{hutchins2020Distributed, hollan2000Distributed}. First, cognitive processes are not confined to individual minds but are distributed across individuals, artifacts, and the environment. Second, technological artifacts are not passive conduits for information but active participants in cognitive processes, shaping how information is represented, stored, and retrieved. Third, the appropriate unit of analysis for understanding cognition is the functional system---the ensemble of people, tools, and representations engaged in a task---rather than the isolated individual. Recent applications have extended distributed cognition beyond its original navigational settings to technology-mediated teamwork. Studies have examined how digital artifacts structure and redistribute cognitive processes in design teams \citep{mangalaraj2014Distributed, nemeth2004Using} and how team cognition co-evolves with cognitive artifact use in project management \citep{drury-grogan2021Changes}. These propositions are particularly relevant to large-scale construction project teams, where knowledge is dispersed across organizational boundaries and DCTs serve as the primary artifacts through which coordination and knowledge mobilization occur \citep{leonardi2015Ambient, safadi2024Balancing}. By treating DCTs as constitutive elements of the cognitive system rather than mere communication channels, the distributed cognition perspective offers a theoretical basis for examining how different patterns of tool use reshape the cognitive resources, specifically meta-knowledge, available to team members.

Within this framework, meta-knowledge functions as a form of cognitive indexing within the distributed system, enabling team members to navigate the knowledge landscape without possessing all relevant expertise themselves \citep{engelbrecht2019How, ye2021Leveraging}. As DCTs become embedded in project work, interaction processes that were previously transient or implicit are externalized through persistent communication traces, visible interaction histories, and routinized collaboration structures \citep{leonardi2014Social, treem2020ComputerMediated}. These externalized traces constitute the informational foundation from which meta-knowledge can develop and be maintained \citep{palazzolo2006Coevolution}. Research suggests that the development of meta-knowledge is closely linked to improvisational action: when team members possess well-developed meta-knowledge, they can rapidly identify, access, and recombine distributed expertise to formulate improvised responses \citep{huang2017Ties, oostervink2016Knowledge}. \subsection{Hypotheses development}
Within the distributed cognition framework, DCTs function as cognitive artifacts that externalize transient interactions into visible traces \citep{treem2013Social, leonardi2014Social}. Different patterns of tool use generate different types of cognitive traces, supplying the distributed system with distinct foundations for meta-knowledge development. We distinguish two complementary externalization modes: work-oriented use externalizes task cognition, while social-oriented use externalizes relational cognition. In large-scale construction projects, cross-organizational workforce composition and multi-site dispersion prevent members from directly observing each other's expertise and work patterns \citep{yang2025How, shen2022Largescale}, making these DCT-mediated cognitive traces the primary foundation for meta-knowledge formation.

\subsubsection{Work-oriented DCT use and meta-knowledge}
Work-oriented use externalizes task-related contributions into accessible cognitive traces, including technical discussions, problem-solving records, and project coordination logs \citep{hadida2015Use, jiang2013Should}. These traces encode content-based information about contributors' expertise domains \citep{lewis2003Measuring}. In large-scale construction projects, members distributed across multiple organizations and work sites cannot directly observe each other's competence through co-located interaction. The cognitive traces externalized through work-oriented use become the primary window into cross-boundary expertise distribution. Through both task-focused search and ambient exposure to accumulated work records \citep{leonardi2015Ambient}, team members can observe what types of expertise different contributors demonstrate and map substantive knowledge domains to specific actors. This content-based expertise mapping supports the development of SM. Accordingly, we propose:

H1a: Work-oriented use is positively associated with SM.

Work-oriented use also makes task interaction patterns visible through traceable digital records: who responds to whom, how requests are routed, and which actors repeatedly coordinate across tasks \citep{borgatti2003Relational, leonardi2017Social}. These externalized interaction patterns reveal the actual coordination relationships that emerge from task execution, enabling team members to infer interpersonal pathways for accessing knowledge beyond what formal organizational charts prescribe \citep{ding2019Enterprise, leonardi2015Ambient}. In large-scale construction projects where formal authority across organizational boundaries provides limited guidance for knowledge access \citep{shen2022Largescale}, the coordination relationships made apparent through work-oriented use become a window into the relational landscape of the project team. By observing recurring patterns, project members can develop awareness of which interpersonal ties serve as effective channels for reaching distributed expertise. Accordingly, we propose:

H1b: Work-oriented use is positively associated with RM.

Work-oriented use externalizes work processes into searchable cognitive traces that constitute what the distributed cognition perspective terms externalized cognitive representations \citep{anand1998Organizational, faraj2000Coordinating}. Task sequence records, coordination histories, and problem-resolution trajectories accumulate as reviewable, searchable artifacts within the distributed cognitive system. In large-scale construction projects, formal process documentation is often incomplete or fragmented across organizational boundaries \citep{yang2025How}, as participating entities maintain different procedural standards and documentation practices. The task execution traces externalized through work-oriented use serve as a de facto procedural cognitive foundation, allowing team members to observe how similar tasks were previously processed, how coordination sequences unfolded, and how problems were resolved across disciplinary boundaries. By reviewing these accumulated process traces, team members develop awareness of procedural patterns and action sequences without relying on prolonged shared experience. Accordingly, we propose:

H1c: Work-oriented use is positively associated with PM.

\subsubsection{Social-oriented DCT use and meta-knowledge}
Complementary to the task-cognition externalization of work-oriented use, social-oriented use externalizes social interactions into perceivable relational traces via DCTs \citep{ali-hassan2015Linking, cao2019Exploring}, supplying the distributed cognitive system with relational and experiential information that work-oriented use cannot generate.

Through social interactions, team members gain exposure to colleagues’ personal backgrounds, informal expertise developed outside current project tasks, and tacit knowledge that remains invisible in formal work exchanges \citep{ma2022Investigating}. Unlike the task-performance signals externalized by work-oriented use, social exchanges reveal experiential and contextual information about competence domains that extend beyond formally assigned roles. In large-scale construction projects, cross-organizational boundaries create information silos about expertise distribution \citep{shen2022Largescale}; social-oriented use provides a complementary channel through which members can discover expertise that would otherwise remain hidden within organizational boundaries. As these social cues accumulate, they enrich and refine members’ awareness of how expertise is distributed across the team, complementing the content-based mapping derived from work-oriented use. Accordingly, we propose:

H2a: Social-oriented use is positively associated with SM.

Social-oriented use externalizes relational structures into perceivable interaction records through informal exchanges \citep{huang2017Ties}. Co-engagement patterns, trust-based ties, and intermediary connections become discernible as social interactions accumulate within DCTs. The distributed cognition perspective holds that the cumulative and traceable nature of DCT-mediated interaction converts transient social exchanges into traceable relational information \citep{leonardi2014Social}. Unlike the formal coordination patterns revealed by work-oriented use, these traces reflect trust-based relationships and personal connections that operate outside established reporting structures. In large-scale construction projects, formal authority structures spanning multiple participating organizations provide limited guidance for cross-boundary knowledge access \citep{shen2022Largescale}. Social-oriented use enables members to discover and map informal relational pathways, identifying which interpersonal ties serve as effective channels for reaching distributed expertise. Accordingly, we propose:

H2b: Social-oriented use is positively associated with RM.

Social-oriented use also externalizes procedural cognition through experience sharing, practical advice, and situational discussion \citep{ali-hassan2015Linking, cao2019Exploring}. These social exchanges provide narrative and judgment-based insights into how work is actually carried out in practice, including tacit norms, preferred approaches to handling recurring issues, and situational judgments about when procedural deviations are acceptable \citep{barmeyer2019Informal}. From a distributed cognition perspective, these informally externalized procedural cues complement the explicit process traces generated by work-oriented use: whereas work-oriented use captures sequential task execution patterns, social-oriented use reveals the informal rules of thumb, workarounds, and contextual adaptations that practitioners apply when formal procedures encounter practical constraints. In large-scale construction projects, formal procedural documentation is often fragmented across participating organizations, and standardized protocols may not fully capture the cross-organizational coordination practices that emerge in actual project delivery. The procedural insights externalized through social-oriented use fill these gaps, helping team members develop awareness of how distributed work processes operate beyond what formal documentation prescribes. Accordingly, we propose:

H2c: Social-oriented use is positively associated with PM.

\subsubsection{Meta-knowledge and improvisation capability}
SM, RM, and PM function as different levels of cognitive indexing that enable the project team to navigate the distributed knowledge landscape without possessing all relevant expertise themselves \citep{engelbrecht2019How, ye2021Leveraging}. Improvisation demands that distributed knowledge be mobilized within compressed timeframes to produce effective responses to unexpected situations \citep{magni2009Role}. The cognitive indexing provided by these three dimensions of meta-knowledge determine both the speed with which relevant knowledge can be located and mobilized, and the breadth of knowledge resources available for recombination. These two mobilization characteristics correspond to the two dimensions of improvisation capability: immediacy and creativity \citep{veranghg2005Improvisation}.

SM enables the project team to identify who possesses relevant domain-specific expertise when unexpected problems arise. In large-scale construction projects, expertise distribution spans multiple organizations and work sites, and the team’s own knowledge is often insufficient to address emergent cross-disciplinary issues. By clarifying the content-based distribution of expertise across team members, SM reduces the cognitive search costs required to locate appropriate knowledge holders \citep{lewis2003Measuring}, enabling rapid identification and mobilization of relevant expertise under time pressure.

SM also facilitates creative responses by allowing the project team to mobilize diverse knowledge domains beyond its immediate task scope \citep{ye2021Leveraging}. In large-scale construction projects, the heterogeneous expertise distributed across disciplinary and organizational boundaries constitutes a rich pool for knowledge recombination. When team members possess awareness of this expertise distribution, they can identify and integrate knowledge elements from different domains, generating solutions that transcend the boundaries of any single discipline. Accordingly, we propose:

H3a: SM is positively associated with immediacy.

H3b: SM is positively associated with creativity.

RM provides awareness of who is connected to whom, which actors serve as intermediaries, and how indirect ties can be activated in practice \citep{huang2017Ties}. This pathway-based awareness shortens the gap between problem recognition and coordinated action \citep{faraj2000Coordinating}. In large-scale construction projects where formal authority across organizational boundaries is limited, RM enables the project team to bypass formal channels and activate interpersonal pathways to reach relevant expertise rapidly.

RM also supports creative responses by facilitating targeted access to knowledge holders across professional boundaries \citep{faraj2000Coordinating}. From a distributed cognition perspective, awareness of interpersonal pathways allows team members to selectively activate specific relational ties to reach expertise holders in different disciplinary domains \citep{perry-smith2017Creativity, soda2019Network}. This targeted cross-boundary interaction exposes team members to different professional logics and knowledge elements outside their own disciplinary repertoire. In large-scale construction projects, RM provides the pathway awareness necessary for team members to reach across organizational and disciplinary boundaries, supporting the recombination of heterogeneous inputs into novel action plans. Accordingly, we propose:

H4a: RM is positively associated with immediacy.

H4b: RM is positively associated with creativity.

PM provides familiar action templates that allow the project team to translate situational cues into action without prolonged deliberation \citep{rulke2000Investigating, turner2022Improving}. From a distributed cognition perspective, PM reduces uncertainty about ``how to act'' by making established action patterns cognitively available as ready-made frameworks for rapid deployment \citep{faraj2000Coordinating}. In emergent situations, delays often stem from uncertainty about how to proceed rather than from a lack of expertise \citep{crossan2003Making}. PM addresses this uncertainty by providing ready-made procedural frameworks, bypassing the need to construct action plans from scratch. In large-scale construction projects, coordination complexity amplifies procedural uncertainty. Team members with well-developed procedural awareness can draw on established coordination sequences to initiate rapid responses, even in unfamiliar situations.

PM supports creative responses by making familiar action patterns available as building blocks for recombination \citep{ciborra1996Improvisation}. When existing procedures prove insufficient for addressing unprecedented contingencies, project teams with well-developed procedural awareness can adapt, hybridize, and recombine known routines to construct novel approaches. In large-scale construction projects, cross-disciplinary procedural awareness expands the repertoire of action templates available for creative recombination, enabling members to draw on a wider range of procedural elements when composing improvised responses. Accordingly, we propose:

H5a: PM is positively associated with immediacy.

H5b: PM is positively associated with creativity.

\subsubsection{The mediating role of meta-knowledge}
DCTs provide the informational foundation for meta-knowledge development. Meta-knowledge, functioning as cognitive indexing, in turn enables rapid mobilization of distributed knowledge to support improvisation. Temporary team composition and limited shared work history suggest that team members cannot rely on gradually accumulated interpersonal familiarity to locate and mobilize distributed expertise \citep{jiang2025Confronting}. Meta-knowledge therefore serves as the cognitive bridge between the information generated through DCT use and the capacity for improvised action. Based on the differential patterns argued above, SM is expected to function as the most consistent mediator, because the visible and retrievable cognitive traces generated by both work-oriented and social-oriented use most directly support content-based expertise mapping, which in turn facilitates both rapid knowledge mobilization (immediacy) and cross-boundary knowledge recombination (creativity).

Based on the direct-effect arguments developed above, SM mediates the work-oriented use--improvisation capability relationship because the task-content traces generated by work-oriented use provide the informational foundation for content-based expertise mapping, which in turn supports both rapid knowledge mobilization and cross-boundary knowledge recombination. RM mediation operates because work-oriented use externalizes task interaction patterns that reveal coordination relationships, enabling pathway-based knowledge access. PM mediation operates because work-oriented use externalizes sequential process traces that make procedural patterns cognitively available as action templates \citep{zhang2018Mobile}. Accordingly, we propose:

H6a: SM mediates the relationship between work-oriented use and improvisation capability.

H6b: RM mediates the relationship between work-oriented use and improvisation capability.

H6c: PM mediates the relationship between work-oriented use and improvisation capability.

For social-oriented use, SM mediation operates because social-oriented use externalizes informal cognitive cues that complement content-based expertise mapping from a different angle than work-oriented use. RM mediation operates because social-oriented use directly externalizes relational cognition, making trust-based pathways and interpersonal connections observable as cognitive artifacts \citep{nisula2016Role}. PM mediation operates because social-oriented use externalizes informal procedural insights from social exchanges, providing tacit norms and practical adaptations that complement formal process awareness. Accordingly, we propose:

H6d: SM mediates the relationship between social-oriented use and improvisation capability.

H6e: RM mediates the relationship between social-oriented use and improvisation capability.

H6f: PM mediates the relationship between social-oriented use and improvisation capability.

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Data collection}
To enhance the reliability and validity of the survey instrument, a pilot study was conducted prior to formal data collection. Using a snowball sampling approach, employees with experience using DCTs in project settings were recruited through the research team's professional networks. A total of 47 valid responses were obtained. The pilot results indicated satisfactory psychometric properties for all measurement scales (Cronbach’s $\alpha$ > 0.70), supporting their suitability for the main study. Prior to formal data collection, an a priori power analysis was conducted using G*Power 3.1 \citep{faul2009Statistical}. Based on a medium effect size ($f^{2}$ = 0.15), a significance level of $\alpha$ = .05, and statistical power of .95, with six predictors in the most complex structural equation, the minimum required sample size was determined to be 146. 

The formal data collection employed a three-wave time-lagged survey design to reduce common method bias \citep{podsakoff2003Common}. Data were collected from participants involved in large-scale construction projects in China, spanning residential buildings, public buildings, infrastructure projects, and industrial buildings. The formal survey was distributed through a Chinese construction training organization that provides government-recognized certification programs for project professionals. The organization's trainee and alumni network includes practitioners from diverse organizational roles and project types, providing access to a broad and heterogeneous sample.

Respondents were required to meet three eligibility criteria: (1) involvement in a project with a total investment of at least US\$100 million, consistent with the definition of large-scale projects proposed by \citet{harrison2024Scale}; (2) at least six months of involvement in their current project; and (3) regular use of digital communication tools for project collaboration. In the Chinese construction context, teams predominantly rely on three digital collaboration platforms, QQ, WeChat, and DingTalk, for daily coordination and communication \citep{zhu2023Dynamics, jia2025Knowledge}. The survey therefore assessed respondents' tool use specifically within these three digital environments.

At Time 1, respondents reported demographic information and DCT usage patterns (work-oriented use and social-oriented use). A total of 567 questionnaires were distributed, and 409 valid responses were returned, yielding an effective response rate of 72.1\%. One month later, the Time 2 survey was administered to respondents who had provided valid responses at Time 1. This wave measured meta-knowledge variables, including structural, relational, and procedural meta-knowledge. A total of 313 valid responses were collected at Time 2, representing a retention rate of 76.5\% relative to the Time 1 sample. Another month later, the Time 3 survey measured improvisation capability, including immediacy and creativity. To match responses across the three waves, respondents were asked to provide the last four digits of their mobile phone numbers as a unique identifier. After matching all three survey waves and excluding cases with missing or inconsistent data, the final sample comprised 255 valid responses, representing an overall retention rate of 62.3\% from Time 1. Respondents who completed all three waves received a gift as compensation for their continued participation. Demographic information of the respondents is presented in Table 2.

To ensure data quality, several measures were implemented. First, the survey company conducted IP address checks to prevent multiple submissions and removed responses with straight-lining patterns or insufficient response time (less than 50\% of median completion time). Second, an attention check question was embedded in the survey to verify that respondents were reading the questions carefully.

\subsection{Bias control and evaluation}
All participants were informed about the purpose of the study, the confidentiality of their responses, and their right to withdraw at any time. Informed consent was obtained from all participants prior to data collection. Several procedural remedies were adopted to mitigate common method bias \citep{podsakoff2003Common}. First, the three-wave design ensured that independent variables, mediators, and dependent variables were measured at separate time points, preventing respondents from inferring the expected relationships among constructs. Following prior research on project-based organizations, a one-month interval was adopted between consecutive survey waves. Research suggests that a time lag of one to six months is appropriate for capturing behavioral and cognitive changes in organizational settings \citep{carmeli2021Resilience, zaman2024Stress}. In this context, one month was deemed sufficient for DCT usage patterns to shape meta-knowledge through repeated daily interaction and coordination cycles, while remaining short enough to ensure that respondents were still engaged in the same ongoing projects, a condition reinforced by the eligibility requirement of at least six months of current project involvement. Second, respondents were assured that their responses were anonymous and would be used solely for academic research purposes. Third, survey instructions explicitly stated that there were no right or wrong answers and encouraged respondents to answer based on their actual experiences.

In addition to these procedural remedies, two statistical tests were conducted to evaluate the severity of common method bias. First, Harman's single-factor test was performed by subjecting all 24 measurement items to an unrotated principal component analysis. The results yielded seven factors with eigenvalues greater than 1.0, with the first factor accounting for 32.38\% of the total variance, well below the 40\% threshold \citep{podsakoff2003Common}. Second, a confirmatory factor analysis compared the hypothesized seven-factor measurement model (see Section 4.1) with a single-factor model in which all items loaded onto one latent factor. The single-factor model exhibited significantly poorer fit ($\chi^{2}$(252) = 1802.07, CFI = 0.496, TLI = 0.448, RMSEA = 0.155, SRMR = 0.124), and the chi-square difference test confirmed the superiority of the seven-factor model ($\Delta\chi^{2}$(21) = 1418.28, p < 0.001). Together, these results suggest that common method bias is unlikely to pose a serious threat to the validity of the findings.

To examine potential non-response bias, the final sample was divided into early and late respondent groups based on the order of Time 1 questionnaire receipt \citep{armstrong1977Estimating}. Independent-samples t-tests and chi-square tests were conducted to compare the two groups on key demographic variables. The results indicated no significant differences between early and late respondents in gender ($\chi^{2}$(1) = 0.476, p = 0.490), age (t = $-$0.502, p = 0.616), educational level ($\chi^{2}$(3) = 1.662, p = 0.645), or work experience (t = 0.055, p = 0.957). These results suggest that non-response bias is not a significant concern in this study.

\subsection{Measures}
All constructs in this study were operationalized using established scales adapted from prior research. To ensure contextual appropriateness and construct equivalence in the Chinese project setting, all measurement items were translated using a back-translation procedure. The translation process involved independent forward translation into Chinese, back-translation into English, and reconciliation by bilingual researchers with expertise in project management and organizational behavior. Discrepancies were discussed and resolved to ensure conceptual consistency with the original scales while maintaining relevance to large-scale project contexts in China. All items were measured using a five-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (“strongly disagree”) to 5 (“strongly agree”). 

Digital communication tool (DCT) usage was measured using eight items adapted from \citet{chen2020Integrating}, including four items capturing work-oriented use (WU) and four capturing social-oriented use (SU). Structural and relational meta-knowledge were each assessed using three-item scales adapted from \citet{engelbrecht2019How}. Procedural meta-knowledge was measured using three items adapted from \citet{faraj2000Coordinating} and modified to reflect the project context. All constructs were measured at the project team level using a key informant approach, in which one knowledgeable project team member reported on behalf of the team. This approach is appropriate given that each respondent was an experienced project professional with direct involvement in day-to-day team interactions and project coordination. Improvisation capability (IC) was measured using seven items from \citet{magni2009Role}, comprising three items for immediacy (IMM) and four for creativity (CRE). All items were adapted to the construction project context and are reported in the Appendix.

Prior research has shown that individual characteristics such as gender, age, and educational background are determinants of IC \citep{koutsoupidou2005Improvisation, magni2009Role}. In addition, project type and professional qualifications may influence both meta-knowledge formation and improvisation capability, as different project contexts present varying levels of complexity and standardization, and different professional roles entail distinct levels of decision-making authority and coordination responsibility. Accordingly, gender, age, educational background, project type, and professional qualifications were included as control variables.


\section{Results}
\subsection{Reliability and validity}
To assess the reliability and validity of the measurement instruments, confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) was conducted using the lavaan package in R \citep{rosseel2012Lavaan}. The seven-factor measurement model demonstrates acceptable fit to the data ($\chi^{2}$(231) = 383.79, $\chi^{2}$/df = 1.661; CFI = 0.947; TLI = 0.936; RMSEA = 0.051, 90\% CI [0.042, 0.060]; SRMR = 0.049). All indices meet commonly accepted thresholds ($\chi^{2}$/df < 3, CFI and TLI > 0.90, RMSEA < 0.08, SRMR < 0.08), indicating adequate model fit.

Internal consistency was assessed using Cronbach's $\alpha$ and composite reliability (CR). As reported in Table 3, Cronbach's $\alpha$ values range from 0.797 to 0.838, and CR values range from 0.816 to 0.860, all exceeding the recommended threshold of 0.70. These results suggest satisfactory internal reliability across all constructs \citep{eisinga2019Building}.

Convergent validity was assessed using the average variance extracted (AVE). As shown in Table 3, AVE values range from 0.527 to 0.672, all exceeding the recommended cutoff of 0.50. This indicates that the measurement items adequately capture the variance of their corresponding latent constructs. Discriminant validity was assessed using the Fornell--Larcker criterion. As presented in Table 3, the square root of the AVE for each construct is greater than its correlations with all other constructs, providing evidence of satisfactory discriminant validity \citep{chen2007Sensitivity, li2019When}.

\subsection{Hypothesis testing}
To test the proposed hypotheses, covariance-based structural equation modeling (CB-SEM) was employed to obtain robust parameter estimates for the research model. The structural model results are presented in Tables 4 and 5, and visualized in Fig. 2.

The results indicate that WU has a significant positive effect on SM ($\beta$ = 0.247, p < 0.001) and PM ($\beta$ = 0.294, p < 0.001), supporting H1a and H1c. However, the effect of WU on RM is not significant ($\beta$ = 0.060, p > 0.05), and thus H1b is not supported. SU shows significant positive effects on SM ($\beta$ = 0.204, p < 0.001) and RM ($\beta$ = 0.193, p < 0.001), supporting H2a and H2b. In contrast, the effect of SU on PM is not significant ($\beta$ = 0.036, p > 0.05), and H2c is not supported.

Regarding the effects of meta-knowledge on IC, SM exhibits significant positive effects on both IMM ($\beta$ = 0.205, p < 0.001) and CRE ($\beta$ = 0.136, p < 0.05), supporting H3a and H3b. RM demonstrates a significant positive association with IMM ($\beta$ = 0.174, p < 0.01), supporting H4a, but its effect on CRE is not significant ($\beta$ = 0.030, p > 0.05), and H4b is not supported. PM shows a significant positive effect on CRE ($\beta$ = 0.256, p < 0.001) but does not significantly affect IMM ($\beta$ = 0.009, p > 0.05). Therefore, H5b is supported, while H5a is not. Full results, including 95\% confidence intervals and standard errors, are reported in Table 4.

Among the control variables, only age showed a significant negative effect on IMM ($\beta$ = $-$0.101, p = 0.031). Gender, educational background, project type, and professional qualifications had no significant effects on either IMM or CRE (all p > 0.05).

Bootstrapping with 5,000 resamples was conducted to assess the significance of indirect effects, and results are reported in Table 5. An indirect effect is considered significant if the 95\% bootstrap confidence interval does not include zero. For the WU pathways, the indirect effects through SM on both IMM ($\beta$ = 0.051) and CRE ($\beta$ = 0.034) are significant, supporting H6a: SM mediates the relationship between WU and IC. In contrast, the confidence intervals for indirect effects through RM include zero for both IMM ($\beta$ = 0.010) and CRE ($\beta$ = 0.002). H6b is thus not supported. For PM, the indirect effect of WU on CRE is significant ($\beta$ = 0.075), whereas the indirect effect on IMM is not ($\beta$ = 0.003). H6c is therefore partially supported: PM mediates the WU--CRE relationship but not the WU--IMM relationship.

For the SU pathways, the indirect effects through SM are significant for both IMM ($\beta$ = 0.042) and CRE ($\beta$ = 0.028). H6d is therefore supported: SM mediates the relationship between SU and IC. The indirect effect through RM is significant for IMM ($\beta$ = 0.034) but not for CRE ($\beta$ = 0.006). H6e is thus partially supported: RM mediates the SU--IMM relationship but not the SU--CRE relationship. For PM, neither the indirect effect on IMM ($\beta$ = 0.001) nor on CRE ($\beta$ = 0.009) is significant. H6f is therefore not supported. The direct effects of both WU and SU on IMM and CRE remain significant after accounting for the mediators (see Table 5), suggesting partial mediation.

\subsection{Robustness checks}
The three-wave time-lagged design employed in this study establishes temporal precedence for the proposed causal direction, as independent variables (T1), mediators (T2), and dependent variables (T3) were measured at separate time points with one-month intervals. Combined with the procedural and statistical common method bias controls reported in Section 3.2, these design features substantially mitigate threats to causal interpretation.

To further assess robustness, we examined whether the core findings are sensitive to the operationalization of improvisation capability. The main analysis modeled immediacy (IMM) and creativity (CRE) as two separate first-order dependent variables, following \citet{magni2009Role}. However, prior research has also operationalized improvisation capability as a higher-order construct \citep{charoensukmongkol2019Entrepreneurial}. We therefore re-estimated the structural model with IC specified as a reflective second-order latent variable measured by IMM and CRE, and re-tested all structural paths and mediation effects without control variables. The second-order model demonstrated good fit ($\chi^{2}$(238) = 332.79, CFI = 0.969, TLI = 0.964, RMSEA = 0.040, SRMR = 0.078). As shown in Table 6, the direction and significance of all six IV-to-mediator paths remained fully consistent with the first-order model: WU significantly predicted SM ($\beta$ = 0.307, p < 0.001) and PM ($\beta$ = 0.344, p < 0.001) but not RM, while SU significantly predicted SM ($\beta$ = 0.232, p < 0.01) and RM ($\beta$ = 0.237, p < 0.05) but not PM. For the mediator-to-IC paths, SM ($\beta$ = 0.255, p < 0.05) and PM ($\beta$ = 0.279, p < 0.01) significantly predicted IC, whereas RM did not ($\beta$ = 0.134, p > 0.05). Bootstrap mediation tests confirmed that the WU--SM--IC ($\beta$ = 0.078), WU--PM--IC ($\beta$ = 0.096), and SU--SM--IC ($\beta$ = 0.059) indirect paths were significant, consistent with the first-order results. The non-significance of the RM--IC path in the second-order model aligns with the first-order findings, where RM significantly predicted IMM but not CRE; aggregating the two dimensions into a single IC construct dilutes this dimension-specific effect. This pattern reinforces the theoretical value of distinguishing between immediacy and creativity as separate facets of improvisation capability. Overall, these results demonstrate that the core findings are robust to the operationalization of the dependent variable.

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{Discussion on the research questions}

\subsubsection{How DCT usage shapes improvisation capability through differentiated pathways (RQ1)}
The empirical results suggest two stages through which DCT usage shape improvisation capability, answering RQ1. First, work-oriented and social-oriented usage cultivate distinct forms of meta-knowledge. Second, meta-knowledge exerts different effects on the project team's immediacy and creativity. 

Regarding the first stage, the results reveal asymmetric patterns in how different DCT usage shape meta-knowledge. Work-oriented use exhibits significant positive effects on structural and procedural meta-knowledge, but not on relational meta-knowledge. Social-oriented use enhances structural and relational meta-knowledge, while showing no significant effect on procedural meta-knowledge. 

These asymmetric meta-knowledge profiles can be explained through the externalization mechanism central to distributed cognition perspective: DCTs convert transient team interactions into retrievable digital traces. Further, the type of trace deposited depends on the orientation of use \citep{treem2020ComputerMediated, leonardi2014Social}. Work-oriented use externalizes task cognition into structured cognitive artifacts that encode information about contributors' expertise domains and the procedural routines  \citep{drury-grogan2021Changes, cho2022Digital}. By accumulating retrievable records of ``who knows what'' and ``how work gets done'', these task-oriented traces provide a rich informational substrate for structural and procedural meta-knowledge \citep{engelbrecht2019How}. However, the traces deposited by work-oriented use carry limited socio-emotional content; they record coordination outcomes but not the interpersonal preferences and collaboration styles from which relational awareness develops \citep{palazzolo2006Coevolution}. Social-oriented use demonstrates a complementary pattern. Rather than depositing task logs, social interactions externalize relational cognition into perceivable digital traces \citep{brockhaus2023Digitalization}. This directly strengthens relational meta-knowledge by making team members' collaboration preferences and interpersonal ties visible \citep{leonardi2015Ambient}. Consistent with \citet{fu2025Enterprise}, the spontaneous and less structured nature of social interaction does not produce process records from which procedural meta-knowledge develops. Through externalization modes, work-oriented and social-oriented use jointly construct a distributed cognitive foundation, while their complementary effects on relational and procedural meta-knowledge reflect different cognitive content \citep{hutchins2020Distributed}.

Regarding the second stage, the results show that the three meta-knowledge dimensions do not all contribute to improvisation capability. Structural meta-knowledge is positively associated with both immediacy and creativity. More nuanced patterns emerge for relational and procedural meta-knowledge. Relational meta-knowledge shows no significant relationship with creativity while procedural meta-knowledge does not exerts a significant positive effect on immediacy.

A knowledge integration lens helps specify the mechanisms underlying the observed asymmetry \citep{perry-smith2017Creativity}. Structural meta-knowledge functions as a general tool that supports both immediacy and creativity by enabling the project team to locate and align relevant expertise regardless of the specific performance demand. Relational and procedural meta-knowledge, in contrast, correspond to more specialized integration pathways. Immediacy relies primarily on rapid mobilization and alignment of relevant expertise; knowing who to turn to under time pressure allows the project team to bypass formal channels and coordinate immediately \citep{okhuysen200910}. This mechanism is amplified in large-scale construction projects, where multiple organizational tiers impose rigid hierarchical approval processes that are too slow to accommodate emergent disruptions \citep{shen2022Largescale}. In such contexts, relational meta-knowledge enables team members to bypass these rigid cross-organizational hierarchies and activate informal trust networks. It secures the rapid response time that immediacy demands \citep{jiang2025Confronting}. Creativity depends more on the recombination of heterogeneous knowledge inputs; the team's shared awareness of work processes provides routines that can be flexibly recombined to generate novel solutions \citep{perry-smith2017Creativity}. Deep cross-disciplinary knowledge heterogeneity in large-scale construction projects intensifies this recombination challenge  \citep{yang2025How}. Procedural meta-knowledge serves not merely as a repository of standard routines but as a boundary-spanning protocol that provides experts from disparate disciplines with a shared procedural language, enabling fragmented knowledge to be rapidly recombined  \citep{xie2025How}. Our findings corroborate \citet{soda2019Network}, who suggests that coordination-oriented interaction structures tend to streamline communication, but may simultaneously reduce exposure to knowledge domains that enable novel recombination. This efficiency--innovation tension explains why relational meta-knowledge supports immediacy but not creativity, and why procedural meta-knowledge supports creativity but not immediacy.

\subsubsection{Meta-knowledge as a selective mediator (RQ2)}
The mediation analysis provides a nuanced answer to RQ2. Meta-knowledge does mediate the relationship between DCT usage and improvisation capability, but the mediation is selective rather than comprehensive. Structural meta-knowledge consistently transmits the effects of DCT use to improvisation capability, whereas relational and procedural meta-knowledge operate as mediators only in specific paths.

The consistency of structural meta-knowledge as a mediator can be explained by the affordances of digital communication environments. DCTs generate highly visible traces of communication and problem solving. Such visibility makes expertise signaling and knowledge location salient, enabling project team members to recognize and update their understanding of expertise distribution through routine digital interaction \citep{chen2020Integrating}. This affordance acquires particular significance in large-scale construction projects, where team members face dual fragmentation---physical dispersion across multiple work sites and organizational dispersion across project participants \citep{shen2022Largescale}. As temporary organizations, these project teams lack the shared work history and tacit mutual understanding that accumulate through long-term collaboration \citep{bakker2010Taking, mariano2024Managing}. In the absence of such pre-existing cognitive foundations, the visible digital traces generated by DCTs become the primary and most efficient pathway through which team members establish ``who knows what'', regardless of whether the interaction is task-focused or socially oriented. Structural meta-knowledge therefore emerges as a dual-pathway mediator because it is reliably supplied by both usage orientations, broadly relevant to both dimensions of improvisation capability, and acutely demanded in project contexts where pre-existing cognitive foundations are absent.

The selective mediation of relational and procedural meta-knowledge reveals what we term a \textit{cognitive-capability alignment} mechanism. From a distributed cognition perspective, digital interaction generates abundant but undifferentiated information flows within the cognitive system \citep{hutchins2020Distributed}. A complete mediation path materializes only when three elements are aligned: the interaction cues that a usage orientation externalizes, the specific cognitive structure (meta-knowledge dimension) that these cues develop, and the capability dimension whose resource integration demands match that cognitive structure. Relational meta-knowledge mediates the social-oriented use--immediacy path because social interaction supplies the socio-emotional cues necessary for relational awareness, and relational awareness in turn supports rapid mobilization under time pressure. However, this path does not extend to creativity, because relational awareness alone does not provide the procedural repertoire needed for creative recombination. Similarly, procedural meta-knowledge mediates the work-oriented use--creativity path because task-focused interaction externalizes work processes, and the team's procedural awareness supports novel recombination. This path does not extend to immediacy, because procedural templates alone are insufficient for immediate alignment under urgency \citep{ren2011Transactive, marion2021Transformation}.

These findings indicate that the cognitive consequences of digital interaction are structured and uneven. Digital communication generates abundant interaction, but only some of this interaction crystallizes into team-level cognitive resources that connect specific usage patterns to specific capability outcomes \citep{ye2021Leveraging, zaverzhenets2021Digitalising}. The cognitive-capability alignment mechanism suggests that prior research reporting inconsistent or contradictory effects of digital collaboration \citep{kane2014Whats} may have overlooked this deeper alignment logic: without differentiating between interaction orientations, meta-knowledge dimensions, and capability demands, the selective nature of mediation remains masked behind aggregate effects. The relationship between digital collaboration and team capability is therefore more contingent than often assumed, governed by the degree of alignment across the externalization--cognition--capability chain. \subsection{Theoretical implications}

This study suggests four theoretical implications for the project management and knowledge management literature. First, this study extends the project improvisation literature by identifying digital communication tool use as a distinct, technology-based antecedent. By establishing that work-oriented and social-oriented usage cultivate distinct forms of meta-knowledge that selectively enable immediacy and creativity, this study introduces a vital technological and cognitive pathway. It demonstrates that in the highly fragmented environment of large-scale construction projects, communication technology is not merely a backdrop but an active enabler that converts distributed expertise into rapid improvisational action.

Second, this study reframes digital communication in project research by showing that digital communication tools can build team cognitive infrastructure instead of merely improving communication efficiency. The findings show that digital communication does not produce uniform effects; instead, its consequences depend on how digital tools are used within project teams. Digital communication therefore operates as a system through which project teams develop the cognitive awareness that enables coordinated action. By shifting attention from technology adoption to interaction orientation, this study positions digital communication as a cognitive infrastructure-building process that underpins team capability in complex project environments \citep{leonardi2015Ambient}.

Third, this study enriches the project management literature by introducing a three-dimensional meta-knowledge framework into a context where it has been largely absent. Project management research, in contrast, has predominantly examined knowledge itself, while paying limited attention to the higher-order awareness of how knowledge is distributed and mobilized. The findings demonstrate that structural, relational, and procedural meta-knowledge each play distinct roles in shaping improvisation capability. These differentiated effects reveal that meta-knowledge is not a monolithic cognitive resource but a multifaceted structure whose dimensions connect to team capability through different pathways. The study extends meta-knowledge theory beyond its traditional organizational boundary into temporary, cross-organizational project settings.

Fourth, the findings extend the application of distributed cognition perspective by showing that team capability depends not only on knowledge possession but also on the ability to mobilize distributed knowledge. Traditional project research has emphasized knowledge acquisition, expertise, and learning as primary drivers of performance, implicitly assuming that teams perform better when members possess more knowledge or higher levels of expertise. The present study shows that, in digitally mediated project environments, the ability to locate, combine, and mobilize distributed knowledge becomes as important as knowledge possession and can be even more critical under time pressure. Project team improvisation capability therefore depends on the team's collective awareness of how knowledge is distributed within the project team, how it can be accessed, and how it is enacted in practice.

\subsection{Practical implications}
This study provides practical insights for project managers seeking to leverage digital communication tools to support improvisation capability. First, the findings reveal that immediacy and creativity follow distinct meta-knowledge pathways. Project managers should conduct differentiated capability assessments before designing team development strategies. This assessment involves two dimensions. At the project-type level, managers should identify which form of improvisation capability their project demands most urgently. Projects characterized by high safety risks, tight schedules, and frequent unexpected disruptions, such as urban tunnel shield boring, place a premium on immediacy. In such contexts, strengthening social-oriented use to cultivate relational meta-knowledge is essential, as knowing whom to contact and how to reach them enables rapid coordinated action. Conversely, projects involving technically complex solutions and multi-disciplinary interfaces, such as complex foundation engineering, demand creativity. Here, reinforcing work-oriented use to develop procedural meta-knowledge is more effective, as the team's shared awareness of standard workflows and approval procedures provides the structured foundation upon which creative recombination can occur. At the project lifecycle level, immediacy needs peak during construction surges and final commissioning phases when disruptions cluster, while creativity needs are highest during early planning, detailed design, and value engineering stages.

Second, project managers should treat digital communication tool usage as a knowledge-building process rather than merely a communication channel. The findings show that specific digital interaction does not generate the full range of meta-knowledge required for improvisation. Project managers should therefore design digital collaboration environments that intentionally combine both task-focused coordination and informal interaction spaces. Modern digital communication platforms such as DingTalk, Feishu, and Microsoft Teams now offer low-code customization capabilities that project teams can leverage to build meta-knowledge-oriented workspaces. For example, teams can create expertise directory pages that tag members by specialty, certification, and prior project experience (supporting structural meta-knowledge), organizational relationship dashboards that visualize cross-unit liaison contacts and collaboration history (supporting relational meta-knowledge), and standardized workflow libraries that catalogue construction procedures and approval sequences (supporting procedural meta-knowledge). Automated onboarding workflows can further support meta-knowledge accumulation by automatically pushing expert directories to team members, thereby reducing the time to develop an adequate understanding of the team's distributed knowledge landscape.

Third, project managers should build project-level distributed cognition systems that use digital communication tools as the hub connecting dispersed knowledge carriers into a searchable and retrievable cognitive infrastructure. Distributed cognition theory posits that cognition resides not solely within individual minds but across the network of people, tools, and artifacts that constitute the working environment. Applying this principle, project organizations can design systems whose components map directly onto the three meta-knowledge dimensions identified in this study, as illustrated in Fig. 3. For structural meta-knowledge, the system should maintain an expert capability atlas. This atlas includes a searchable repository documenting each participating organization's specializations, professional qualifications, key personnel, and relevant project experience, so that team members can rapidly locate the right expertise when unexpected problems arise. For relational meta-knowledge, the system should capture the collaboration network---cross-organizational liaison contacts, historical cooperation records, and communication frequency logs---enabling teams to identify trusted coordination channels under time pressure. For procedural meta-knowledge, the system should house a procedure knowledge base containing standardized construction sequences, emergency response plans, construction method templates, variation approval workflows, and relevant regulatory policies. Beyond these three pillars, the system should incorporate project decision logs that record the rationale behind key technical choices, a lessons-learned repository that archives post-incident reviews and project retrospectives, and real-time alert mechanisms that push critical updates to relevant personnel when conditions change.

\subsection{Limitations and future research directions}
This study has several limitations that should be acknowledged. First, the proposed meta-knowledge pathways were examined within large-scale construction project context. These features make meta-knowledge critical for coordination and improvisation. However, the prominence of meta-knowledge observed in this study may be partly context-specific. In projects with more stable membership, higher task modularity, or stronger technological integration, such as software development or R\&D teams, DCT usage may generate different cognitive consequences. Future research can examine how project characteristics shape the relative importance of different meta-knowledge pathways and explore the boundary conditions of the proposed framework across diverse project settings.

Second, all team-level constructs were measured using a single key informant per project team. While this approach is well-established in organizational research and is appropriate when informants have comprehensive knowledge of team processes, it cannot directly verify whether other team members would report similar perceptions. Future research could collect data from multiple members per team to examine within-team consensus and strengthen the validity of team-level construct measurement.

Third, the empirical data were collected exclusively from large-scale construction project teams in China. China's construction industry exhibits distinctive institutional and cultural characteristics that may shape how DCT use influences meta-knowledge development. At the same time, the theoretical mechanisms underpinning the proposed model---the externalization of task and relational cognition through DCT use and the resulting development of team-level meta-knowledge---are grounded in distributed cognition principles that are not inherently tied to a particular national context. Large-scale construction projects in other countries similarly involve dispersed expertise, cross-organizational boundaries, and time-compressed coordination demands, suggesting that the core pathways identified in this study are likely to extend beyond the Chinese context. However, caution is warranted when generalizing the findings to other national contexts, particularly those with different digital tool ecosystems and contrasting cultural orientations toward knowledge sharing. Future research should examine whether these pathways replicate in different institutional and cultural contexts.

\section{Conclusion}
This study examines how digital communication tool use shapes large-scale construction project teams’ improvisation capability through meta-knowledge development. Drawing on distributed cognition perspective, the study develops and tests a theoretical model that distinguishes work-oriented and social-oriented DCT usage and conceptualizes structural, relational, and procedural meta-knowledge as key cognitive mechanisms linking digital interaction to team capability. The model is examined using time-lagged survey data collected from members of large-scale construction project teams.

The findings reveal that DCT usage does not influence improvisation capability uniformly but operates through differentiated meta-knowledge pathways. Work-oriented and social-oriented DCT usage foster distinct yet complementary forms of meta-knowledge, while structural meta-knowledge emerges as the most consistent mechanism linking digital collaboration to both dimensions of improvisation capability. Relational and procedural meta-knowledge exhibit more selective effects, highlighting the uneven cognitive consequences of digital interaction. Together, these results demonstrate that the impact of DCT usage depends on how digital interaction reshapes team cognitive infrastructures rather than on technology use alone.

These insights contribute to research on digital collaboration, distributed cognition, and project teamwork by clarifying the cognitive mechanisms through which digital interaction translates into team capability. The findings also offer guidance for project organizations seeking to design digital collaboration practices that support both rapid response and creative problem solving in complex project environments. Because the empirical context focuses on construction and infrastructure project teams in China, caution is warranted when generalizing the findings to other project settings and national contexts.
